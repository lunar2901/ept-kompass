## Why this concept exists

Half of all points lost on engineering Klausuren come from one source: a unit that wasn't converted. You compute a force in kg·m/s² but the answer sheet wants N. You use pressure in bar where the formula needs Pa. You convert kilowatt-hours to joules wrong. These aren't mistakes of understanding — they are mistakes of attention.

This lesson is a field guide to the unit system you will use for the next six years. The goal is not to learn new physics; it is to build the habit of writing every number with its unit attached, converting everything to SI at the start of a problem, and checking units before you check arithmetic.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The seven SI base units

<FormulaCard
  name_de="SI-Grundeinheiten"
  name_en="SI base units"
>

| Size | Symbol | German name | English name |
| --- | :---: | --- | --- |
| Length | m | Meter | metre |
| Mass | kg | Kilogramm | kilogram |
| Time | s | Sekunde | second |
| Temperature | K | Kelvin | kelvin |
| Amount of substance | mol | Mol | mole |
| Electric current | A | Ampere | ampere |
| Luminous intensity | cd | Candela | candela |

Every other SI unit is derived from these seven. For engineering, the first four and the sixth (mol for chemistry) dominate.

</FormulaCard>

### Derived units you'll use daily

| Size | Unit | In base units |
| --- | :---: | --- |
| Force | N (Newton) | kg·m/s² |
| Pressure | Pa (Pascal) | N/m² = kg/(m·s²) |
| Energy | J (Joule) | N·m = kg·m²/s² |
| Power | W (Watt) | J/s = kg·m²/s³ |
| Frequency | Hz (Hertz) | 1/s |
| Electrical charge | C (Coulomb) | A·s |
| Voltage | V (Volt) | W/A = kg·m²/(A·s³) |
| Resistance | Ω (Ohm) | V/A |

### SI prefixes

  name_de="SI-Vorsätze"
  name_en="SI prefixes"
>

| Prefix | Symbol | Factor |
| --- | :---: | --- |
| tera | T | 10¹² |
| giga | G | 10⁹ |
| mega | M | 10⁶ |
| kilo | k | 10³ |
| hekto | h | 10² |
| deka | da | 10¹ |
| **1** | — | 10⁰ |
| dezi | d | 10⁻¹ |
| centi | c | 10⁻² |
| milli | m | 10⁻³ |
| mikro | μ | 10⁻⁶ |
| nano | n | 10⁻⁹ |
| pico | p | 10⁻¹² |

</FormulaCard>

Three traps for engineering students:

- **k (kilo) vs K (kelvin).** Lowercase k is 10³; uppercase K is kelvin (a unit, not a prefix). `kW` = kilowatt; `K` = kelvin. Klausuren will test this.
- **m (milli) vs M (mega).** Case matters! `mW` (milliwatt = 10⁻³ W) vs `MW` (megawatt = 10⁶ W). Off by a factor of 10⁹.
- **"kg" is the base unit, not "g".** Annoying historical quirk — the base unit for mass is the kilogram. So "milligram" (mg) is 10⁻⁶ of the base unit (kg), not 10⁻³.

### Common non-SI units engineers meet

| Unit | Where used | SI equivalent |
| --- | --- | --- |
| bar | pressure (meteorology, engineering) | 10⁵ Pa |
| atm | pressure (chemistry, old textbooks) | 101 325 Pa |
| mmHg, Torr | pressure (medicine, vacuum) | 133.3 Pa |
| L, Liter | volume | 10⁻³ m³ |
| kWh | electrical energy (billing) | 3.6·10⁶ J |
| eV | atomic/nuclear energy | 1.602·10⁻¹⁹ J |
| kcal | food, old heat-transfer | 4184 J |
| °C | temperature (everyday) | K - 273.15 |

<Callout variant="warning">

**Celsius and Kelvin have the same scale size but different zero points.**

$T[\mathrm{K}] = T[\mathrm{°C}] + 273{,}15$.

For *differences* or *ratios*, a 1 K change equals a 1 °C change — so you can skip the conversion if only $\Delta T$ matters. For *absolute* temperatures (as in $pV = nRT$), you MUST convert to K.

A student who plugs 27 °C into $T$ of the ideal-gas law will get answers about an order of magnitude wrong. The habit: *always write K in your formula. Always convert at input.*

</Callout>

### Dimensional analysis as a cheat-check

For every formula, the two sides must have the same units. If a problem gives you:

$$F = m \cdot a$$

and you have $m$ in kg, $a$ in m/s², then $F$ has units of kg·m/s² = N. ✓

Any mismatch (kg·m/s and you forgot the squared) is a flag. Engineers develop the habit of writing units after every number, canceling/combining them alongside the algebra, and checking the final unit matches what was asked.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Die spezifische Enthalpie von Dampf beträgt $h = 2{,}75\,\mathrm{MJ/kg}$. Der Massenstrom sei $\dot{m} = 250\,\mathrm{kg/h}$. Wie groß ist der Enthalpiestrom $\dot{H}$ in **Kilowatt**?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Step 1. Convert all inputs to SI.**

- $h = 2{,}75\,\mathrm{MJ/kg} = 2{,}75 \cdot 10^6\,\mathrm{J/kg}$
- $\dot{m} = 250\,\mathrm{kg/h} = 250 / 3600\,\mathrm{kg/s} = 0{,}0694\,\mathrm{kg/s}$

**Step 2. Multiply.**

$$\dot{H} = \dot{m} \cdot h = 0{,}0694\,\mathrm{kg/s} \cdot 2{,}75 \cdot 10^6\,\mathrm{J/kg}$$

$$\dot{H} = 190\,972\,\mathrm{J/s} = 190\,972\,\mathrm{W}$$

**Step 3. Convert to kW.**

$$\dot{H} \approx 191\,\mathrm{kW}$$

**Cross-check.** 1 kg/h of 2.75 MJ/kg steam = 2.75 MJ/h = 2.75·10⁶ J / 3600 s ≈ 764 W. For 250 kg/h: 250 × 764 ≈ 191 000 W = 191 kW. ✓

</details>

  question_en="An immersion heater has a power of 2.2 kW. How many joules does it consume in 30 seconds?"

  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: 66 000 J = 66 kJ.**

$$E = P \\cdot t = 2{,}2\\,\\mathrm{kW} \\cdot 30\\,\\mathrm{s} = 2200\\,\\mathrm{W} \\cdot 30\\,\\mathrm{s}$$

$$E = 66\\,000\\,\\mathrm{W \\cdot s} = 66\\,000\\,\\mathrm{J} = 66\\,\\mathrm{kJ}$$

Good mental check: 1 W = 1 J/s, so 1 kW × 1 s = 1 kJ. 2.2 kW × 30 s → 66 kJ.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Forgetting kW → W: 2.2 · 30 = 66 J",
      why_en: "Factor of 1000 off. 2.2 kW = 2200 W, not 2.2. When the problem gives kW, you almost always need to multiply by 1000 to get into base SI."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**The J / Ws / kWh hierarchy.**

- 1 J = 1 W·s — the base SI energy unit.
- 1 Ws = 1 J (same thing, different notation).
- 1 kWh = 1000 W · 3600 s = 3.6·10⁶ J = 3.6 MJ.

A 2 kW heater running for 1 hour uses 2 kWh = 7.2 MJ. Your household electric bill is in kWh because J/s over hours produces huge numbers.

Every engineering calculation should use J internally. Convert kWh to J at the start, report in kWh at the end if the context requires. Never work with mixed units in the middle of a calculation.
  `}

<Exercise
  id="track-0-si-l1-ex3"
  difficulty="medium"
  type="numeric"
  question_de="Ein Auto fährt 80 km/h. Wie groß ist die Geschwindigkeit in m/s?"
  question_en="A car drives 80 km/h. What is the speed in m/s?"
  correct={22.22}
  tolerance={0.1}
  hint_en="1 km = 1000 m. 1 h = 3600 s. Multiply by (1000/3600) = 10/36."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $\\approx 22{,}22\\,\\mathrm{m/s}$.**

$$80 \\,\\frac{\\mathrm{km}}{\\mathrm{h}} = 80 \\cdot \\frac{1000\\,\\mathrm{m}}{3600\\,\\mathrm{s}} = \\frac{80\\,000}{3600}\\,\\mathrm{m/s}$$

$$= \\frac{80}{3{,}6}\\,\\mathrm{m/s} \\approx 22{,}22\\,\\mathrm{m/s}$$

**Short cut.** Divide km/h by 3.6 to get m/s. Multiply m/s by 3.6 to get km/h. This is worth memorising — engineering problems often mix km/h (speeds) with m/s (flow velocities) and you need the conversion cold.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Forgetting to convert time: 80 · 1000 / 60 ≈ 1333 m/s",
      why_en: "You need TO convert BOTH 'km → m' AND 'h → s'. Missing either gives a number 60× or 1000× off. The combined factor is 1000/3600 = 1/3.6 ≈ 0.278."
    },
    {
      wrong: "Multiplying instead of dividing: 80 · 3.6 = 288 m/s",
      why_en: "Going the wrong way. km/h are BIGGER numerically than m/s (since there are many m per km but also many s per h, and the km-per-m ratio wins). So km/h → m/s gives SMALLER numbers: divide. 22 m/s is about 80 km/h; 288 m/s would be the speed of sound."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why engineers stick with m/s.**

SI formulas use m/s because the base unit of length is m and of time is s. Every kinematic quantity — velocity, acceleration, momentum — is naturally m/s, m/s², kg·m/s. Mixing km/h into a calculation is asking for trouble.

Typical engineering speed contexts:
- air flow in ducts: 5-20 m/s
- water in pipes: 1-5 m/s
- steam in pipes: 30-80 m/s
- car: 30 m/s (≈108 km/h)
- sound in air: 343 m/s
- jet engine exhaust: 500 m/s

For thermodynamics, the typical "nominal fluid velocity" in turbines and nozzles is hundreds of m/s, so kinetic energy terms $c^2/2$ can matter: $(300\\,\\mathrm{m/s})^2/2 = 45\\,000\\,\\mathrm{J/kg} = 45\\,\\mathrm{kJ/kg}$, which is ~5% of typical $\\Delta h$ values.
  `}
/>

  solution_mdx={`
**Correct answer: (c).**

Strictly speaking, (c) is arithmetically correct: "kK" means "kilokelvin" = 1000 K. But in practice, no one writes "kK" — temperatures in engineering never span a thousand-fold range where 1000 K would be a convenient base.

The deeper issue: the Kelvin scale has physical zero at 0 K (absolute zero), and everyday engineering temperatures are in the 200-2000 K range. Prefixing with 'kilo' would mean measuring everyday temperatures in 'millikelvin' (0.3 kK for room temperature), which is ridiculous.

So (c) is the "wrong" answer in the sense that the statement is arithmetically right but practically nonsense — and the question is asking you to notice that.

**Options (a), (b), (d) are all correct** and useful conversion facts. Note (b) especially: mg → kg requires two steps (×10⁻³ then ×10⁻³), because the base unit is kg, not g.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "a or d — these are correct arithmetic facts",
      why_en: "Both (a) and (d) state standard SI relationships. If you picked one, double-check the prefix values: M = 10⁶, k = 10³."
    },
    {
      wrong: "b — correct: milligram to kilogram",
      why_en: "1 mg = 10⁻³ g = 10⁻³ · 10⁻³ kg = 10⁻⁶ kg. Two factors because the base unit for mass is kg, not g. This is a quirky historical quirk of SI."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why "kg" is the only prefixed base unit.**

Historical: when the SI system was codified (1960), the kilogram was already the base — defined by the international prototype kilogram artifact. It couldn't be re-defined as "gram" without invalidating all existing measurements. So we're stuck with "kilogram" as a base unit that already has a prefix.

Practical consequence: when converting mass within SI, you need to count prefixes carefully. 1 mg = 10⁻³ g = 10⁻⁶ kg. 1 Mg = 10⁶ g = 10³ kg (which is 1 metric ton, though usually written "t").

**Prefixes that are unusual but valid.**

- Pascal: engineers use kPa, MPa, GPa (structural) — all valid.
- Joule: J, kJ, MJ, GJ are all commonly used. PJ (petajoule, 10¹⁵ J) for national energy statistics.
- Watt: W, kW, MW, GW are standard (power of plants, national capacity).
- Second: µs, ns, ps in electronics; ks, Ms rarely used (we say "hours" instead).

A good rule of thumb: choose the prefix that keeps the numerical value between 0.1 and 1000. If your answer is 0.001 kW, rewrite as 1 W. If it's 5 000 000 J, rewrite as 5 MJ.
  `}

<Exercise
  id="track-0-si-l1-ex5"
  difficulty="klausur"
  type="numeric"
  question_de="Ein Heizkörper gibt Q = 15 kWh pro Tag ab. Wie groß ist die durchschnittliche Heizleistung P (in W)?"
  question_en="A radiator delivers Q = 15 kWh per day. What is the average heating power P (in W)?"
  correct={625}
  tolerance={3}
  hint_en="Power = Energy / Time. Convert kWh → J and day → s. Or: divide kWh by 24 h = kW, then × 1000."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $P \\approx 625\\,\\mathrm{W}$.**

**Method 1: direct conversion.**

$$P = \\frac{Q}{t} = \\frac{15\\,\\mathrm{kWh}}{24\\,\\mathrm{h}} = 0{,}625\\,\\mathrm{kW} = 625\\,\\mathrm{W}$$

(Because kWh / h = kW. The hours cancel.)

**Method 2: full SI conversion.**

$$Q = 15\\,\\mathrm{kWh} \\cdot 3{,}6 \\cdot 10^6\\,\\mathrm{J/kWh} = 5{,}4 \\cdot 10^7\\,\\mathrm{J}$$

$$t = 24 \\cdot 3600\\,\\mathrm{s} = 86\\,400\\,\\mathrm{s}$$

$$P = \\frac{5{,}4 \\cdot 10^7}{86\\,400} = 625\\,\\mathrm{J/s} = 625\\,\\mathrm{W}$$

Both methods give the same answer. Method 1 is faster once you see the kWh / h → kW trick.

**Context.** 625 W average is a small radiator — typical for a small bathroom. A living-room radiator is more like 1500-2000 W average in winter.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Dividing kWh by 24 and reporting 0.625 as the answer",
      why_en: "The answer is in W, and 0.625 kW = 625 W. If you stopped at 0.625, you forgot the unit conversion at the end. Always check the question's required unit."
    },
    {
      wrong: "Dividing kWh by 86 400 s without converting to J first",
      why_en: "kWh / s gives nonsense units (energy per second, but in weird prefixes). Convert kWh to J first if you're using seconds. Or use method 1: stay in kWh/h and get kW directly."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why Method 1 is elegant.**

The units of 1 kWh = 1 kW · 1 h by definition. So kWh / h = kW · h / h = kW. The units of time cancel cleanly and you get straight to kW without ever computing in J.

General principle: when a rate unit already exists (kWh, km/h, kg/h), sometimes dividing by time in the same prefix is faster than converting to SI. But ALWAYS verify the final unit matches what's requested.

**Energy vs Power — the dimension pair.**

- Energy [E] = power × time [P·T]
- Power [P] = energy / time [E/T]

Every problem that gives you one and asks for the other requires a time conversion. The most common mistake is confusing the two — "energy of a 100 W bulb" is unbounded (depends how long it's on); "power of 15 kWh" is not a meaningful question without a time duration.
  `}

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** Write a two-column **Umrechnungstabelle** at the start of every problem:

| Gegeben (as stated) | In SI |
| --- | --- |
| $p = 3\,\mathrm{bar}$ | $p = 3 \cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{Pa}$ |
| $T = 20\,\mathrm{°C}$ | $T = 293{,}15\,\mathrm{K}$ |
| $\dot{m} = 2\,\mathrm{kg/h}$ | $\dot{m} = 5{,}56 \cdot 10^{-4}\,\mathrm{kg/s}$ |
| ... | ... |

Only use the right column for calculations. This single habit prevents ~half of Klausur unit errors.

</Callout>
