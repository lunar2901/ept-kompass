## Why this concept exists

If differentiation is "slope of a function", integration is "area under a function". The two operations are inverses — the *Hauptsatz der Differential- und Integralrechnung* (fundamental theorem of calculus) makes it formal.

You will meet integrals constantly in engineering:

- <GermanTerm de="Volumenänderungsarbeit" article="die" en="boundary work" /> $W = -\int p\,\mathrm{d}V$ — area under the $p$–$V$ curve.
- <GermanTerm de="Wärmemenge" article="die" en="heat quantity" /> $Q = \int \dot{Q}\,\mathrm{d}t$ — area under the heat-flow-rate curve.
- Total mass flowing through a pipe: $m = \int \dot{m}\,\mathrm{d}t$.
- Internal-energy change: $\Delta U = \int c_v(T)\,m\,\mathrm{d}T$.

This lesson rebuilds integration from its geometric meaning, then equips you with the rules to compute most engineering integrals mechanically.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Two kinds of integral

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Unbestimmtes Integral (Stammfunktion)"
  name_en="Indefinite integral (antiderivative)"
  latex="\int f(x) \, \mathrm{d}x = F(x) + C"
>

$F(x)$ is any function with $F'(x) = f(x)$; $C$ is the *Integrationskonstante*, an arbitrary constant that gets lost in differentiation.

</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Bestimmtes Integral"
  name_en="Definite integral"
  latex="\int_a^b f(x) \, \mathrm{d}x = F(b) - F(a)"
>

The *Hauptsatz* says: to compute the definite integral, find any antiderivative $F$ and evaluate at the endpoints. The arbitrary constant $C$ cancels: $[F(b) + C] - [F(a) + C] = F(b) - F(a)$.

Geometrically: the signed area between the curve and the x-axis, from $x = a$ to $x = b$. Signed because portions where $f(x) < 0$ count as negative area.

</FormulaCard>

### The rules — inverse of differentiation

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Grundintegrale"
  name_en="Basic integrals"
>

| Function $f(x)$ | Antiderivative $\int f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x$ |
| --- | --- |
| $x^n$ ($n \neq -1$) | $\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C$ |
| $1/x$ | $\ln|x| + C$ |
| $e^x$ | $e^x + C$ |
| $e^{ax}$ | $\dfrac{e^{ax}}{a} + C$ |
| $\sin x$ | $-\cos x + C$ |
| $\cos x$ | $\sin x + C$ |
| constant $c$ | $c\,x + C$ |

</FormulaCard>

**Linearity of integration.** For constants $a$, $b$ and functions $f$, $g$:

$$\int (a\,f(x) + b\,g(x))\,\mathrm{d}x = a\int f(x)\,\mathrm{d}x + b\int g(x)\,\mathrm{d}x$$

Integrals distribute over sums and constants pull out — just like derivatives.

### What about chain rule and product rule? ⇒ Substitution and partial fractions

The integration analogues of chain rule and product rule are:

- <GermanTerm de="Substitution" article="die" en="substitution" /> ($u = g(x)$) — the inverse of the chain rule.
- <GermanTerm de="Partielle Integration" article="die" en="integration by parts" /> — the inverse of the product rule: $\int u'v\,\mathrm{d}x = uv - \int uv'\,\mathrm{d}x$.

These are more art than recipe. Learn them when you need them; for first-semester engineering, the basic integrals + linearity cover 80 % of problems.

### The geometric interpretation: area

If $f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a, b]$, then $\int_a^b f\,\mathrm{d}x$ is the area between the curve and the x-axis. Specific engineering readings:

- On a $p$–$V$ diagram, $-\int p\,\mathrm{d}V$ is the boundary work — area under the $p(V)$ curve.
- On a $\dot{Q}$–$t$ plot, $\int \dot{Q}\,\mathrm{d}t$ is the total heat transferred — area under the heat-flow-rate curve.
- On a $\dot{m}$–$t$ plot, $\int \dot{m}\,\mathrm{d}t$ is the total mass that has flowed — area under the mass-flow-rate curve.

<Callout variant="note">

**Units check.** The integral of a rate quantity over time gives a total quantity with matching units. $\int [W]\,\mathrm{d}[s] = [W\cdot s] = [J]$ — so $\int \dot{Q}\,\mathrm{d}t$ is in J, as expected for heat.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Berechnen Sie:
>
> a) $\int (3x^2 - 2x + 1) \, \mathrm{d}x$
> b) $\int_0^2 4x \, \mathrm{d}x$
> c) $\int_1^5 \frac{1}{x} \, \mathrm{d}x$

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Indefinite integral.** Linearity: integrate each term using the power rule, and the constant using the constant rule.

$$\int 3x^2\,\mathrm{d}x = 3 \cdot \frac{x^3}{3} = x^3$$
$$\int (-2x)\,\mathrm{d}x = -2 \cdot \frac{x^2}{2} = -x^2$$
$$\int 1\,\mathrm{d}x = x$$

Sum: $x^3 - x^2 + x + C$.

**b) Definite integral.** Antiderivative of $4x$: $F(x) = 2x^2$. Evaluate:

$$\int_0^2 4x\,\mathrm{d}x = F(2) - F(0) = 2(4) - 0 = 8$$

Geometric check: triangle with base 2 and height $4 \cdot 2 = 8$, area $= \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 \cdot 8 = 8$. ✓

**c) Definite log integral.**

$$\int_1^5 \frac{1}{x}\,\mathrm{d}x = [\ln|x|]_1^5 = \ln 5 - \ln 1 = \ln 5 - 0 = \ln 5 \approx 1{,}609$$

A number worth recognising — $\ln 5 \approx 1{,}6$ and $\ln 10 \approx 2{,}3$ both appear frequently in engineering.

</details>

      wrong: "Getting 12 + C (leaving C in a definite integral)",

**The Hauptsatz der Differential- und Integralrechnung.**

$$\\int_a^b F'(x)\\,\\mathrm{d}x = F(b) - F(a)$$

This single identity reduces integral calculation to:

1. Find any antiderivative.
2. Evaluate at upper bound minus lower bound.

Both steps are mechanical once you have a catalogue of basic integrals. The challenge is step 1 for complicated integrands — that's where substitution, integration by parts, and partial-fraction decomposition come in.

**Sign conventions.** The definite integral from $a$ to $b$ can be negative if $b < a$ or if $f$ is negative over the interval. In engineering:

- $\\int p\\,\\mathrm{d}V$ is positive for expansion (V going from V₁ to V₂ > V₁), giving positive area under a positive $p$ curve.
- The closed-system boundary-work sign convention puts a minus in front: $W = -\\int p\\,\\mathrm{d}V$, so positive-area expansion gives negative $W$ (work OUT of system).

Always verify the integration bounds match the physical direction of the process.
  `}

**Why integration is essential for time-varying rates.**

In engineering, energies, masses, and charges are TOTAL quantities; powers, flow rates, and currents are RATES. The fundamental relationship:

$$\\text{total} = \\int \\text{rate}\\,\\mathrm{d}t$$

If the rate is constant, this reduces to rate × time — multiplication. If the rate varies, you must integrate. For an exponentially decaying rate, the integral shows up in:

- Capacitor discharge: $Q_{lost}(t) = Q_0 (1 - e^{-t/RC})$, asymptotically approaching $Q_0$.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For any time-varying engineering rate problem (energy, mass, heat) where you need a total quantity:

1. Write down the integral form first: $Q = \int \dot{Q}\,\mathrm{d}t$.
2. Identify the functional form of the rate (constant, linear, exponential, etc.).
3. Apply the matching basic integral from the table.
4. Plug in bounds; verify the final unit makes sense.

For constant rates (most Thermodynamik I Klausur problems), the integral reduces to rate × time. For time-varying rates, the integral is essential — and the basic exponential form covers ~90 % of them.

</Callout>
