## Why this concept exists

Every oscillation, every phase angle, every rotating machine's angular position, every AC circuit waveform, every wave in fluid mechanics — all are described with sine and cosine. The most efficient way to understand them both is the <GermanTerm de="Einheitskreis" article="der" en="unit circle" />: a circle of radius 1 on which a point's $x$-coordinate is $\cos\theta$ and $y$-coordinate is $\sin\theta$.

This lesson rebuilds the unit-circle picture, the special-angle values you need to know by heart, and the pair of identities that show up in every engineering derivation involving phases.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The unit circle — one picture says it all

A circle of radius 1 centred at the origin. A point on the circle at angle $\theta$ from the positive $x$-axis has coordinates:

$$(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$$

So $\cos$ and $\sin$ are literally the $x$ and $y$ coordinates of a unit-circle point. This single picture tells you:

- $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ (from the Pythagorean theorem — $x^2 + y^2 = r^2 = 1$)
- $\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta$ (reflecting across x-axis flips y but not x)
- $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta$
- Both are periodic with period $2\pi$ (going around the circle brings you back)

### Degrees vs radians

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Bogenmaß vs. Gradmaß"
  name_en="Radian measure vs. degree measure"
  latex="\theta[\mathrm{rad}] = \theta[\mathrm{°}] \cdot \frac{\pi}{180}"
>

Calculators have a mode switch — make sure it matches the problem. In physics and engineering formulas, radians are the default (because $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ for small $\theta$ in radians). In everyday problems, degrees are more common.

**Round-trip reference.** $360° = 2\pi\,\mathrm{rad}$. So $180° = \pi$, $90° = \pi/2$, $60° = \pi/3$, $45° = \pi/4$, $30° = \pi/6$.

</FormulaCard>

### The special-angle values — memorise this table

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Wichtige Sinus- und Kosinuswerte"
  name_en="Key sine and cosine values"
>

| $\theta$ | 0° | 30° | 45° | 60° | 90° |
| --- | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| rad | 0 | π/6 | π/4 | π/3 | π/2 |
| $\sin\theta$ | 0 | 1/2 | $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | 1 |
| $\cos\theta$ | 1 | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$ | $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ | 1/2 | 0 |

Pattern: moving left-to-right, $\sin$ values are $\frac{\sqrt{n}}{2}$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4$; $\cos$ values are the reverse.

Memorising this row is worth the 20 minutes. It saves time on a Klausur every single time a 30°, 45°, or 60° angle appears.

</FormulaCard>

### Two identities that earn their keep

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Pythagoreische Identität"
  name_en="Pythagorean identity"
  latex="\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1"
>

Rewritten: $\sin^2\theta = 1 - \cos^2\theta$, $\cos^2\theta = 1 - \sin^2\theta$. Use these to convert expressions involving $\sin^2$ into $\cos^2$ and vice versa.

</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Additionstheoreme"
  name_en="Sum formulas"
  latex="\sin(\alpha \pm \beta) = \sin\alpha \cos\beta \pm \cos\alpha \sin\beta"
>

$$\cos(\alpha \pm \beta) = \cos\alpha \cos\beta \mp \sin\alpha \sin\beta$$

Specialised forms you will see often:

- $\sin(2\alpha) = 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha$ (double-angle)
- $\cos(2\alpha) = \cos^2\alpha - \sin^2\alpha = 1 - 2\sin^2\alpha = 2\cos^2\alpha - 1$
- $\cos(\alpha - \beta) - \cos(\alpha + \beta) = 2\sin\alpha\sin\beta$ (used in wave interference)

</FormulaCard>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Berechnen Sie ohne Taschenrechner:
>
> a) $\sin(60°)$
> b) $\cos(\pi/4)$
> c) $\sin^2(30°) + \cos^2(30°)$

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a)** From the table: $\sin(60°) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 0{,}866$.

**b)** $\pi/4 = 45°$, and from the table: $\cos(45°) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \approx 0{,}707$.

**c)** Pythagorean identity: $\sin^2(30°) + \cos^2(30°) = 1$ regardless of the specific angle. No computation needed.

</details>

**When to replace cos with sin or vice versa.**

- Integrating $\\cos^2(x)$: use the identity $\\cos^2(x) = \\frac{1 + \\cos(2x)}{2}$ (derivable from the double-angle formula) to get a clean integral.
- Simplifying expressions in wave mechanics: often one substitution eliminates a term entirely.
- Fourier-series coefficients: convert all products of sines and cosines into sums via product-to-sum formulas (further identities you'll meet in Analysis II).

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** When a problem uses angles in degrees, check your calculator's mode BEFORE plugging in. A radian-mode calculator evaluating $\sin(90)$ gives 0.894, not 1 — an error that ripples through an entire problem. The mode switch takes one second; fixing the downstream arithmetic takes five minutes.

</Callout>
