<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this exists

You have not used a logarithm since school. That is fine. You will meet them again roughly ten minutes into your first Thermodynamik lecture (entropy of an ideal gas), and again in *Reaktionstechnik* (reaction rates), in *Regelungstechnik* (Bode plots), and in *Verfahrenstechnik* (pH, decibels, orders of magnitude). So let us fix it now, once, properly.

A logarithm answers one specific question: **"to what power do I have to raise this base to get that number?"** That is it. Everything else — the log rules, the graph shape, the "natural log" — follows from that single question.

</Callout>

## Der eine Gedanke, den du nie vergessen darfst

Everything about logarithms reduces to this:

$$
\log_b(x) = y \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad b^y = x
$$

Read the symbol $\Longleftrightarrow$ as "is the same question as". So $\log_{10}(1000) = 3$ is the same question as $10^3 = 1000$. A logarithm is literally **the exponent that the base is raised to**.

That is why engineers use logs when quantities span many orders of magnitude. A concentration of $10^{-7}$ and a concentration of $10^{-3}$ look almost the same next to each other on a normal axis. Take $-\log_{10}$ and they become $7$ and $3$ — neat, readable, and that is exactly what *pH-Wert* is.

| $\ln(x)$ | <GermanTerm de="natürlicher Logarithmus" article="der" plural="natürliche Logarithmen" en="natural log" /> | $e \approx 2.718$ | thermodynamics, reaction kinetics, anything with $e^x$ |

One warning. In a German textbook *"log"* without a subscript almost always means $\log_{10}$. In an English textbook and in most scientific calculators, *"log"* without a subscript still means $\log_{10}$, **but** in pure mathematics papers it often means $\ln$. **In any TU Berlin engineering exam, write $\ln$ or $\lg$ explicitly. Never write bare *log*. You will lose marks for ambiguity.**

## Die Rechenregeln — log rules, derived from the one idea

You do not need to memorise these as separate facts. They all come from the fact that a log is an exponent, and exponents combine by addition when you multiply powers.

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Logarithmus eines Produkts"
  name_en="Log of a product"
  latex="\log_b(x \cdot y) = \log_b(x) + \log_b(y)"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "b", name_de: "Basis", name_en: "base", unit: "—" },
    { symbol: "x, y", name_de: "positive reelle Zahlen", name_en: "positive real numbers", unit: "—" },
  ]}
>
Why: if $x = b^p$ and $y = b^q$, then $x \cdot y = b^{p+q}$, so the exponent of the product is $p + q = \log_b(x) + \log_b(y)$.
</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Logarithmus eines Quotienten"
  name_en="Log of a quotient"
  latex="\log_b\!\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b(x) - \log_b(y)"
>
Same logic: dividing powers subtracts exponents.
</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Logarithmus einer Potenz"
  name_en="Log of a power"
  latex="\log_b(x^n) = n \cdot \log_b(x)"
>
$x^n = (b^p)^n = b^{np}$, so the exponent is $n \cdot p$.
</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Basiswechsel"
  name_en="Change of base"
  latex="\log_b(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(b)} = \frac{\lg(x)}{\lg(b)}"
>
Your calculator only has *ln* and *lg*. If you need $\log_2(50)$, compute $\ln(50) / \ln(2)$. That is the change-of-base formula doing its job.
</FormulaCard>

## Zwei Werte, die du auswendig kennen solltest

- $\ln(1) = 0$ — because $e^0 = 1$.
- $\ln(e) = 1$ — because $e^1 = e$.
- $\lg(1) = 0$, $\lg(10) = 1$, $\lg(100) = 2$, $\lg(1000) = 3$.

In a Klausur you do not always have a calculator for every step. Recognising that $\ln(e^3) = 3$ without reaching for the calculator saves you time you will need later.

## Musterrechnung

> **Aufgabe.** Vereinfachen Sie: $\ln(e^2 \cdot x^3) - \ln(x)$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösung (English walk-through)**</summary>

**Step 1.** Apply the product rule to the first term:
$$\ln(e^2 \cdot x^3) = \ln(e^2) + \ln(x^3)$$

**Step 2.** Apply the power rule to both pieces:
$$= 2 \ln(e) + 3 \ln(x) = 2 \cdot 1 + 3 \ln(x) = 2 + 3 \ln(x)$$

**Step 3.** Subtract $\ln(x)$:
$$2 + 3\ln(x) - \ln(x) = 2 + 2\ln(x)$$

**Answer:** $2 + 2\ln(x)$, or equivalently $2 + \ln(x^2)$.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** When you see a log in a formula, look first at its argument. If the argument is a **ratio** (like $p_1/p_2$), the units inside cancel — you can plug in bar, Pa, or atm, as long as you use the same unit on top and bottom. This saves you a unit-conversion step and is the source of half the arithmetic mistakes on thermodynamics exams.

</Callout>
