## Why this concept exists

Every quantity with a direction — force, velocity, acceleration, electric field, heat flux — is a <GermanTerm de="Vektor" article="der" en="vector" />. To work with them you need to add them, scale them, project one onto another, and find perpendiculars. This lesson gives you the three operations that handle all of that: addition, scalar product (dot product), and cross product.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### What is a vector

A 3D vector $\vec{a}$ is written in columns (or as a triple):

$$\vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{or} \quad (a_1, a_2, a_3)$$

The **magnitude** (length) is:

$$|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}$$

Unit vectors $\hat{a}$ have magnitude 1: $\hat{a} = \vec{a} / |\vec{a}|$.

### Addition and scalar multiplication

Component-wise:

$$\vec{a} + \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 + b_1 \\ a_2 + b_2 \\ a_3 + b_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\lambda \vec{a} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda a_1 \\ \lambda a_2 \\ \lambda a_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Geometrically: addition is "tail to head" (parallelogram rule); scalar multiplication stretches (for positive $\lambda$) or reverses (for negative $\lambda$).

### Scalar product (dot product)

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Skalarprodukt"
  name_en="Dot product"
  latex="\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3 = |\vec{a}||\vec{b}| \cos\theta"
>

Two equivalent formulas — one algebraic (components), one geometric (angle between vectors).

**Uses:**

- Angle between vectors: $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|}$.
- Check perpendicularity: $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 0 \Leftrightarrow \vec{a} \perp \vec{b}$.
- Projection of $\vec{a}$ onto $\vec{b}$: length is $\vec{a} \cdot \hat{b} = (\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})/|\vec{b}|$.
- Work: $W = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{s}$ — force dotted with displacement.

</FormulaCard>

### Cross product (vector product)

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Kreuzprodukt"
  name_en="Cross product"
  latex="\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 \\ a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3 \\ a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1 \end{pmatrix}"
>

The result is a **vector** perpendicular to both $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$, with magnitude $|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|\sin\theta$ (the area of the parallelogram spanned by the vectors). Direction from the right-hand rule.

**Uses:**

- Normal to a plane containing $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$: $\vec{a} \times \vec{b}$.
- Area of a parallelogram: $|\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|$.
- Torque: $\vec{M} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}$ — position crossed with force.
- Angular momentum: $\vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p}$.

**Non-commutative:** $\vec{a} \times \vec{b} = -(\vec{b} \times \vec{a})$.

</FormulaCard>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Gegeben sind $\vec{a} = (1, 2, 3)$, $\vec{b} = (2, 0, -1)$.
>
> a) Berechnen Sie $\vec{a} + \vec{b}$.
> b) Berechnen Sie $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}$.
> c) Berechnen Sie den Winkel zwischen $\vec{a}$ und $\vec{b}$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Sum.** $\vec{a} + \vec{b} = (1+2, 2+0, 3-1) = (3, 2, 2)$.

**b) Dot product.** $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = 1 \cdot 2 + 2 \cdot 0 + 3 \cdot (-1) = 2 + 0 - 3 = -1$.

**c) Angle.** Magnitudes: $|\vec{a}| = \sqrt{1+4+9} = \sqrt{14} \approx 3{,}74$; $|\vec{b}| = \sqrt{4+0+1} = \sqrt{5} \approx 2{,}24$.

$\cos\theta = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{14}\sqrt{5}} = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{70}} \approx -0{,}120$.

$\theta = \arccos(-0{,}120) \approx 96{,}9°$ (slightly obtuse).

</details>

**The Pythagorean theorem in 3D.**

For a right triangle in 2D: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. In 3D, the length of a space diagonal of a box is $d^2 = a^2 + b^2 + c^2$. Vector magnitude generalises this to any orientation.

**Engineering consequence.** Any vector quantity (displacement, velocity, force) has a magnitude computed this way. The 3-4-5 example is common because many textbook problems use such triples to get clean integer answers.

**Answer: -3.**

$$\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b} = 2 \\cdot 4 + (-1) \\cdot 5 + 3 \\cdot (-2) = 8 - 5 - 6 = -3$$

Negative dot product means the vectors point into "opposite half-spaces" (angle > 90°).
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Forgetting a sign: 8 - 5 + 6 = 9 or 8 + 5 - 6 = 7",
      why_en: "Three component multiplications, three signs to track. Slow down: $2 \\cdot 4 = 8$ (positive), $(-1) \\cdot 5 = -5$ (the minus belongs to the first factor, so product is negative), $3 \\cdot (-2) = -6$ (minus from second factor). Always check each sign."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**The sign of the dot product tells you the angle type.**

- Positive: angle < 90° (vectors "point the same way").
- Zero: angle = 90° (perpendicular).
- Negative: angle > 90° ("pointing away from each other").

**In mechanics.** Work done by a force on a body is $W = \\vec{F} \\cdot \\vec{s}$. If the force is in the direction of motion (e.g. pushing a cart forward), $W > 0$. If opposite (friction), $W < 0$. If perpendicular (e.g. gravity on horizontal motion), $W = 0$. The dot-product sign IS the energy-transfer sign.
  `}
/>

  id="track-0-vec-l1-ex3"
  difficulty="medium"
  type="multiple_choice"
  question_de="Zwei Vektoren a und b sind senkrecht zueinander. Welche Bedingung gilt?"
  question_en="Two vectors a and b are perpendicular. Which condition holds?"
  options={[
    { id: "a", de: "a · b = 0", en: "a · b = 0" },
    { id: "b", de: "|a| = |b|", en: "|a| = |b|" },
    { id: "c", de: "a × b = 0", en: "a × b = 0" },
    { id: "d", de: "a + b = 0", en: "a + b = 0" },
  ]}
  correct="a"
  hint_en="Perpendicular means angle = 90°, and cos(90°) = 0 enters the dot-product formula."
  solution_mdx={`
**Correct answer: (a) $\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b} = 0$.**

From $\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b} = |\\vec{a}||\\vec{b}|\\cos\\theta$: if $\\theta = 90°$, then $\\cos\\theta = 0$, so the dot product vanishes.

- **(b)** Equal magnitudes doesn't mean perpendicular — could be parallel or at any angle.
- **(c)** $\\vec{a} \\times \\vec{b} = 0$ means PARALLEL (the cross product vanishes when the angle is 0 or 180°).
- **(d)** $\\vec{a} + \\vec{b} = 0$ means ANTIPARALLEL (b = -a), not perpendicular.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "c — cross product is zero",
      why_en: "Opposite of truth. $|\\vec{a} \\times \\vec{b}| = |\\vec{a}||\\vec{b}|\\sin\\theta$. Perpendicular (90°) makes sin = 1, so cross product has MAXIMUM magnitude, not zero. Zero cross product means parallel (sin = 0)."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Dot product vs cross product — summary.**

| Operation | Result type | Zero when | Max when |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $\\vec{a} \\cdot \\vec{b}$ | scalar | $\\theta = 90°$ (perpendicular) | $\\theta = 0$ (parallel) |
| $\\vec{a} \\times \\vec{b}$ | vector | $\\theta = 0$ or $180°$ (parallel/antiparallel) | $\\theta = 90°$ |

They are complementary tests: the dot product detects parallelism's absence (perpendicularity); the cross product detects parallelism itself.

**In engineering.** Checking whether a force is perpendicular to a surface: dot product of force with surface normal. If dot = 0, force is along the surface — no normal stress, pure shear. This one test shows up in stress analysis, fluid mechanics (no-slip condition), and electrostatics (conductor surface).
  `}
/>

<Exercise
  id="track-0-vec-l1-ex4"
  difficulty="klausur"
  type="short_derivation"
  question_de="Berechnen Sie das Kreuzprodukt a × b für a = (1, 0, 0) und b = (0, 1, 0)."
  question_en="Compute the cross product a × b for a = (1, 0, 0) and b = (0, 1, 0)."
  correct_latex="(0, 0, 1)"
  hint_en="Standard basis vectors. Use the component formula, or recognise e_x × e_y = e_z from the right-hand rule."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $(0, 0, 1) = \\hat{e}_z$.**

**Component formula:**

$$\\vec{a} \\times \\vec{b} = \\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\cdot 0 - 0 \\cdot 1 \\\\ 0 \\cdot 0 - 1 \\cdot 0 \\\\ 1 \\cdot 1 - 0 \\cdot 0 \\end{pmatrix} = \\begin{pmatrix} 0 \\\\ 0 \\\\ 1 \\end{pmatrix}$$

**Right-hand rule.** Point fingers along $\\vec{a}$ ($x$-axis), curl toward $\\vec{b}$ ($y$-axis). Thumb points in $+z$. Result: $\\hat{e}_z = (0, 0, 1)$.

**The three cyclic identities.**

$$\\hat{e}_x \\times \\hat{e}_y = \\hat{e}_z$$
$$\\hat{e}_y \\times \\hat{e}_z = \\hat{e}_x$$
$$\\hat{e}_z \\times \\hat{e}_x = \\hat{e}_y$$

(Reversed ordering negates the result: $\\hat{e}_y \\times \\hat{e}_x = -\\hat{e}_z$.)

These three identities form the basis of every cross-product calculation in 3D. Memorise the cyclic pattern x → y → z → x (positive) vs x → z → y (negative).
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Answering (1, 1, 0) or similar — summing as if it were a dot product",
      why_en: "Cross product is NOT component sum. It's a specific determinant-like formula giving a vector perpendicular to both inputs. The x and y components of $e_x \\times e_y$ are zero; only z is non-zero."
    },
    {
      wrong: "(0, 0, -1) (right-hand rule confusion)",
      why_en: "$\\hat{e}_x \\times \\hat{e}_y = +\\hat{e}_z$ (positive). The REVERSE, $\\hat{e}_y \\times \\hat{e}_x$, gives $-\\hat{e}_z$. Order matters — cross product is anti-commutative. If you consistently get the wrong sign, you may be using a left-handed coordinate system (rare in engineering; physics is right-handed)."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why the right-hand rule.**

Conventionally, $(x, y, z)$ coordinates are right-handed: point your right hand's fingers along $+x$, curl toward $+y$, and your thumb points in $+z$. This convention makes $\\hat{e}_x \\times \\hat{e}_y = \\hat{e}_z$ (not $-\\hat{e}_z$).

Left-handed systems exist in some computer graphics (screen-coordinate systems), but for physics and engineering we always use right-handed.

**In engineering:**
- Torque: $\\vec{M} = \\vec{r} \\times \\vec{F}$ — position from pivot crossed with force direction.
- Angular velocity: $\\vec{\\omega}$ is oriented along the rotation axis via the right-hand rule.
- Magnetic Lorentz force: $\\vec{F} = q\\vec{v} \\times \\vec{B}$.

All rely on the $x \\to y \\to z$ cyclic pattern.
  `}
/>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For cross products, memorise the cyclic pattern $\hat{e}_x \times \hat{e}_y = \hat{e}_z$ (and its two cyclic partners). Combined with the component formula (or the determinant mnemonic), you can compute any cross product reliably. Slow down for sign — anti-commutativity means every swap flips the answer.

</Callout>
