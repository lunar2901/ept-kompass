## Why this concept exists

The first derivative tells you the *slope* of a function. The second derivative tells you the slope of the slope — whether the function is curving up or down. Once you have both, you can find the highest and lowest points of any smooth function mechanically, without guessing. That procedure — the <GermanTerm de="Kurvendiskussion" article="die" en="curve discussion / critical-point analysis" /> — is standard Klausur fare and the mathematical backbone of optimisation everywhere in engineering.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Higher derivatives

Once you have $f'(x)$, you can differentiate it again to get $f''(x)$ — the second derivative. And again: $f'''$, $f^{(4)}$, and so on. Notation:

$$f'(x), \quad f''(x) = \frac{\mathrm{d}^2 f}{\mathrm{d}x^2}, \quad f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{\mathrm{d}^n f}{\mathrm{d}x^n}$$

Physical meanings:

- Position $s(t)$ → velocity $s'(t) = v(t)$ → acceleration $s''(t) = a(t)$.
- For small vibrations, $s(t) = A\cos(\omega t)$ gives $s''(t) = -\omega^2 s(t)$ — the equation of every harmonic oscillator.

### The shape of a function — concavity

The second derivative tells you the concavity:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Konvex, konkav, Wendepunkt"
  name_en="Convex, concave, inflection point"
>

- **$f''(x) > 0$**: function is **konvex** (linksgekrümmt) — curves upward like a cup.
- **$f''(x) < 0$**: function is **konkav** (rechtsgekrümmt) — curves downward like a hill.
- **$f''(x_0) = 0$ with a sign change**: $x_0$ is a **Wendepunkt** — the curvature flips.

A classic example: $f(x) = x^3$. Derivative $f'(x) = 3x^2 \geq 0$ always, so it is monotonically increasing. Second derivative $f''(x) = 6x$ — negative for $x < 0$, zero at $x = 0$, positive for $x > 0$. So $x = 0$ is a Wendepunkt where the curve switches from concave to convex.

</FormulaCard>

### Finding maxima and minima

Every local extremum of a differentiable function $f$ on the interior of its domain satisfies $f'(x) = 0$. These are the **critical points** (*kritische Stellen*). But $f'(x) = 0$ is necessary, not sufficient — a critical point can be a maximum, a minimum, OR a saddle-like horizontal inflection.

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Hinreichende Bedingung für Extrema"
  name_en="Sufficient condition for extrema"
>

Let $f'(x_0) = 0$. Then:

- **$f''(x_0) > 0$** → $x_0$ is a **Minimum** (local low point, $f$ curves up).
- **$f''(x_0) < 0$** → $x_0$ is a **Maximum** (local high point, $f$ curves down).
- **$f''(x_0) = 0$** → test inconclusive; check higher derivatives or the sign of $f'$ around $x_0$.

</FormulaCard>

### The three-step recipe

To find all extrema of a function $f$ on an interval:

1. **Compute $f'(x)$ and solve $f'(x) = 0$** — gives you candidate points.
2. **Evaluate $f''(x)$ at each candidate** — positive means min, negative means max.
3. **Check the boundary** of the interval too — endpoints can be the extremum even if $f' \neq 0$ there (on a closed interval).

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For optimisation word problems (*Extremwertaufgaben*):

1. Identify the *objective* you're optimising — often area, volume, cost, efficiency.
2. Write it as a function $f$ of ONE variable, using constraints to eliminate others.
3. Apply the recipe. Check the answer makes physical sense (positive volume, meaningful dimensions).

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Bestimmen Sie alle lokalen Extrema und Wendepunkte der Funktion $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 2$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Step 1. First derivative.**

$$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x - 2)$$

Setting $f'(x) = 0$: candidates are $x = 0$ and $x = 2$.

**Step 2. Second derivative.**

$$f''(x) = 6x - 6 = 6(x - 1)$$

Evaluate at each candidate:

- $f''(0) = -6 < 0$ → **Maximum** at $x = 0$. $f(0) = 2$.
- $f''(2) = +6 > 0$ → **Minimum** at $x = 2$. $f(2) = 8 - 12 + 2 = -2$.

**Step 3. Wendepunkt.** $f''(x) = 0$ at $x = 1$. Check third derivative: $f'''(x) = 6 \neq 0$, so $x = 1$ is indeed a Wendepunkt (not a degenerate double-zero).

$f(1) = 1 - 3 + 2 = 0$. So the inflection point is at $(1, 0)$.

**Summary:** Maximum at $(0, 2)$, Minimum at $(2, -2)$, Wendepunkt at $(1, 0)$.

</details>

**What the second derivative tells you physically.**

For motion problems, $s''(t) = a(t)$ is the acceleration. Units: m/s². Zero acceleration means constant velocity — free-coasting. Positive acceleration means speeding up in the positive direction.

For cost functions $C(x)$ of production volume $x$, $C''(x)$ is the "curvature" — economists call it the rate at which marginal cost changes. If $C''(x) > 0$, you're in the regime of increasing marginal cost (more production means proportionally more expensive), which matters for deciding optimal production levels.

For stress-strain curves in materials, the second derivative of stress with respect to strain captures non-linear behaviour in the plastic regime.

**Connection to the Taylor series.** The Taylor expansion of $f$ around $x_0$:

$$f(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0) + \\tfrac{1}{2} f''(x_0)(x-x_0)^2 + O(x-x_0)^3$$

So the second derivative is exactly the coefficient of the quadratic correction — it's how a function deviates from being linear near any point.

**When the second-derivative test fails — higher derivatives.**

There's a generalisation: if $f'(x_0) = f''(x_0) = \\dots = f^{(n-1)}(x_0) = 0$ but $f^{(n)}(x_0) \\neq 0$:

- $n$ even AND $f^{(n)}(x_0) > 0$: minimum.
- $n$ even AND $f^{(n)}(x_0) < 0$: maximum.
- $n$ odd: horizontal inflection (neither extremum).

For $f(x) = x^4$ at 0: $f'(0) = f''(0) = f'''(0) = 0$, $f^{(4)}(0) = 24 > 0$. $n = 4$ is even and the fourth derivative is positive → minimum. ✓

- $f'$ changes from $-$ to $+$: minimum.
- $f'$ changes from $+$ to $-$: maximum.
- $f'$ keeps the same sign: horizontal inflection (NOT an extremum).

This works even when higher-derivative tests become unwieldy, and it's often faster for Klausur problems.

**Connection forward.** In multivariable calculus (Analysis II), the second-derivative test becomes the **Hessian matrix** — eigenvalues tell you min/max/saddle. The same logic scales up: the second derivative is *the* tool for classifying critical points, no matter how many variables.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp for Kurvendiskussion.** When asked to "analyse" or "diskutieren" a function, the standard checklist is:

1. Definitionsbereich (domain)
2. Nullstellen ($f(x) = 0$)
3. Symmetrie (gerade, ungerade, keins)
4. Verhalten für $x \to \pm\infty$
5. Erste Ableitung und Extrema
6. Zweite Ableitung und Wendepunkte
7. Skizze

Write down the whole list at the start and tick items off — no forgotten steps, structured presentation, examiner-friendly.
