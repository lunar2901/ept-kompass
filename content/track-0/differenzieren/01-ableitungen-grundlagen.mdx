## Why this concept exists

The moment a physics formula has a dot on top of a variable — $\dot{m}$, $\dot{Q}$, $\dot{V}$ — or contains a "rate of change", you are looking at a derivative. Ingenieure arbeiten den ganzen Tag mit Ableitungen. Luckily, differentiation is a *technique* more than a theory: a short list of rules that, applied mechanically, handles every practical formula.

This lesson rebuilds your intuition from scratch: what a derivative IS, how to compute it using three basic rules, and what it means when it appears in engineering formulas like $\dot{Q} = k A \frac{\mathrm{d}T}{\mathrm{d}x}$.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The intuition: slope of a tangent

At any point $x$ on a curve $f(x)$, imagine zooming in so far that the curve looks like a straight line. The **slope** of that line is the **derivative** of $f$ at $x$:

$$f'(x) = \frac{\mathrm{d}f}{\mathrm{d}x}$$

Read: "the derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$." The notation $\mathrm{d}f/\mathrm{d}x$ (Leibniz) and $f'(x)$ (Lagrange) mean the same thing; you will see both.

Physical examples:

- Position $s(t)$ vs time $t$: the derivative $\frac{\mathrm{d}s}{\mathrm{d}t} = v$ is velocity.
- Velocity $v(t)$: $\frac{\mathrm{d}v}{\mathrm{d}t} = a$ is acceleration.
- Mass $m(t)$ of a leaking tank: $\frac{\mathrm{d}m}{\mathrm{d}t} = -\dot{m}_{\text{leak}}$.
- Temperature along a rod, $T(x)$: $\frac{\mathrm{d}T}{\mathrm{d}x}$ is the temperature gradient, which drives heat flow.

### The three rules you need cold

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Konstanten-Regel"
  name_en="Constant rule"
  latex="\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(c) = 0, \quad \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(c \cdot f(x)) = c \cdot f'(x)"
>

Constants have zero slope. Constant multiples factor out of derivatives.

</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Potenzregel"
  name_en="Power rule"
  latex="\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(x^n) = n \cdot x^{n-1}"
>

Works for any real $n$, not just integers. Examples:

- $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(x^2) = 2x$
- $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(x^{1/2}) = \frac{1}{2} x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$
- $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(1/x) = \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(x^{-1}) = -x^{-2} = -\frac{1}{x^2}$

</FormulaCard>

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Summenregel"
  name_en="Sum rule"
  latex="\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}(f(x) + g(x)) = f'(x) + g'(x)"
>

Derivatives distribute over sums (and, by the same logic, differences).

</FormulaCard>

### Special functions — memorise these

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Wichtige Grundableitungen"
  name_en="Important basic derivatives"
>

| Function | Derivative |
| --- | --- |
| $\sin(x)$ | $\cos(x)$ |
| $\cos(x)$ | $-\sin(x)$ |
| $\tan(x)$ | $\sec^2(x) = 1/\cos^2(x)$ |
| $e^x$ | $e^x$ (its own derivative — this is why $e$ is special) |
| $\ln(x)$ | $1/x$ |
| $a^x$ | $a^x \ln(a)$ |
| $\log_a(x)$ | $1/(x \ln a)$ |

</FormulaCard>

### Kettenregel (chain rule) — for compositions

For any composition $f(g(x))$:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Kettenregel"
  name_en="Chain rule"
  latex="\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)"
>

"Outer derivative × inner derivative." Examples:

- $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} (x^2 + 1)^5 = 5(x^2 + 1)^4 \cdot 2x$
- $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} \sin(3x) = \cos(3x) \cdot 3$
- $\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} e^{-\alpha t} = -\alpha \cdot e^{-\alpha t}$ (with $x \to t$, a mainstay of kinetics)

</FormulaCard>

### Partielle Ableitungen — in thermodynamics

For functions of multiple variables like $U(T, V)$, the **partial derivative** fixes all but one variable:

$$\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial T}\right)_V = \text{rate of change of } U \text{ with } T, \text{ at constant } V$$

The subscript $V$ says "V is held constant". This is the definition of $c_v$:

$$c_v = \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial T}\right)_v$$

and of $c_p$:

$$c_p = \left(\frac{\partial h}{\partial T}\right)_p$$

<Callout variant="note">

Partial derivatives are just ordinary derivatives where you treat the other variables as if they were constants. Learn to set them up correctly — the subscript telling you "at constant X" matters.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Differenzieren Sie:
>
> a) $f(x) = 3x^4 - 2x^2 + 5$
> b) $g(t) = e^{-2t} \sin(t)$ (Hinweis: Produktregel)
> c) $\eta(T) = 1 - \frac{T_{\text{kalt}}}{T}$ (Carnot-Wirkungsgrad, $T_{\text{kalt}}$ konstant)

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Power + sum rule.**

$$f'(x) = 3 \cdot 4 x^3 - 2 \cdot 2x + 0 = 12x^3 - 4x$$

**b) Product rule** ($\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t}(uv) = u'v + uv'$) with $u = e^{-2t}$, $v = \sin(t)$:

$$g'(t) = (-2 e^{-2t}) \sin(t) + e^{-2t} \cos(t) = e^{-2t}(\cos t - 2\sin t)$$

**c) Carnot efficiency derivative.**

Write $\eta = 1 - T_{\text{kalt}} T^{-1}$. Then:

$$\frac{\mathrm{d}\eta}{\mathrm{d}T} = 0 - T_{\text{kalt}} \cdot (-1) T^{-2} = \frac{T_{\text{kalt}}}{T^2}$$

Interpretation: the Carnot efficiency rises with $T$ (hot reservoir temperature), but the rate of improvement decreases like $1/T^2$. Doubling $T$ only improves efficiency by a small amount once $T$ is already high. This is why gas turbines keep pushing $T$ up despite materials challenges — each extra 100 K matters less than the previous.

</details>

From the limit definition $f'(x) = \\lim_{h \\to 0} \\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$, applying to $x^n$ gives a binomial expansion where all higher-order terms in $h$ vanish as $h \\to 0$, leaving just $n x^{n-1}$. Detailed derivation is a first-year calculus exercise.

- Flow rate vs pipe length with quadratic pressure drop.
- Temperature profile along a heat exchanger (often quartic in reality).
- Voltage across a resistor network with mixed linear / quadratic elements.

  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $g'(x) = -3 e^{-3x}$.**

Chain rule with $f(u) = e^u$, $u = -3x$:

- Outer derivative: $\\frac{\\mathrm{d}}{\\mathrm{d}u}(e^u) = e^u$
- Inner derivative: $\\frac{\\mathrm{d}}{\\mathrm{d}x}(-3x) = -3$

Product: $e^{-3x} \\cdot (-3) = -3 e^{-3x}$.

**Meta-pattern:** $\\frac{\\mathrm{d}}{\\mathrm{d}x} e^{ax} = a \\cdot e^{ax}$ for any constant $a$. Worth memorising — it appears in every kinetics and decay problem.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Answering $e^{-3x}$ (forgot the chain-rule multiplier)",
      why_en: "For $e^x$, the derivative is $e^x$ — true, but only when the exponent is exactly $x$. For $e^{-3x}$, the chain rule multiplies by $-3$. This is a classic early mistake; always ask 'what's the inner function?'"
    },
    {
      wrong: "Answering $-3 e^{-3x-1}$ or similar (misapplying the power rule)",
      why_en: "The power rule ($x^n$) doesn't apply to $e^u$ — they have different derivative rules. $e^{-3x}$ means 'e to the exponent $-3x$', not 'e to the power of $-3x-1$'. The derivative of $e^u$ is always $e^u$ itself, just multiplied by the inner derivative."
    }

  discussion_mdx={`
**Why $e^x$ and its friends matter so much in engineering.**

Exponentials with negative exponents describe **decay**: radioactive isotopes, discharging capacitors, cooling towards ambient temperature (Newton's law of cooling), reaction kinetics (Arrhenius).

Positive exponents describe **growth**: population, compound interest, initial-stage exponential feedback in many instabilities.

In both cases the characteristic form is $f(t) = f_0 \\, e^{\\pm t/\\tau}$, where $\\tau$ is the time constant. Taking the derivative:

$$\\frac{\\mathrm{d}f}{\\mathrm{d}t} = \\pm \\frac{1}{\\tau} f_0 e^{\\pm t/\\tau} = \\pm \\frac{f(t)}{\\tau}$$

So the instantaneous rate of change is proportional to the current value — the defining property of exponential processes. This is why the differential equation $\\mathrm{d}f/\\mathrm{d}t = \\pm f/\\tau$ has exponential solutions: it's a self-similar process.

**In thermodynamics.** The Arrhenius equation $k = A e^{-E_A/(RT)}$ gives reaction rate as a function of temperature. Differentiating wrt $T$ using the chain rule gives the temperature sensitivity — essential for understanding why reactions speed up exponentially with heat.
  `}

**The product rule in thermodynamics — deriving dH = dU + d(pV).**

Starting from $H = U + pV$ and taking differentials:

$$\\mathrm{d}H = \\mathrm{d}U + \\mathrm{d}(pV) = \\mathrm{d}U + p\\,\\mathrm{d}V + V\\,\\mathrm{d}p$$

The product rule is hiding in plain sight: $\\mathrm{d}(pV) = p\\,\\mathrm{d}V + V\\,\\mathrm{d}p$. This identity is used every time you derive the open-system first law from the closed-system version.

**Product rule in open-system energy balance.**

For mass flow in an open system, the energy balance has terms like $\\frac{\\mathrm{d}(m u)}{\\mathrm{d}t}$ where both $m$ (mass in CV) and $u$ (specific internal energy) can change. Product rule:

$$\\frac{\\mathrm{d}(mu)}{\\mathrm{d}t} = m \\frac{\\mathrm{d}u}{\\mathrm{d}t} + u \\frac{\\mathrm{d}m}{\\mathrm{d}t}$$

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** A differentiation problem where you freeze up is usually a chain-rule or product-rule problem in disguise. Two questions:

1. Is my function a COMPOSITION $f(g(x))$? If yes, chain rule.
2. Is my function a PRODUCT $f(x) \cdot g(x)$? If yes, product rule.

If neither, it's likely a sum of basic pieces — apply the power, exponential, or log rule to each piece separately. Practice until these identifications are second nature.

</Callout>
