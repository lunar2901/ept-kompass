## Why this concept exists

You were good at fractions when you were 12, and then you stopped using them. Engineering formulas are saturated with them: $\frac{\dot{m}\,(h_2 - h_1)}{\eta}$ or $\frac{p\,V}{R\,T}$ or $\frac{1}{\tau} = \frac{1}{R\,C}$. Ten seconds of fraction arithmetic should not be the thing that breaks your flow on a Klausur. Let's make sure it isn't.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The four operations

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Grundrechenarten mit Brüchen"
  name_en="Basic fraction arithmetic"
>

**Addition / Subtraktion** — need a common denominator:

$$\frac{a}{b} \pm \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a\,d \pm b\,c}{b\,d}$$

**Multiplikation** — straightforward:

$$\frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a\,c}{b\,d}$$

**Division** — multiply by the reciprocal:

$$\frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{d}{c} = \frac{a\,d}{b\,c}$$

**Reciprocal (Kehrwert):** flip numerator and denominator.

$$\frac{1}{a/b} = \frac{b}{a}$$

</FormulaCard>

### Simplification — always reduce at the end

A fraction is "simplified" when numerator and denominator share no common factor. Divide both by the GCD. Example: $\frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$ (divided both by 6).

### Engineering habit: keep fractions symbolic

Resist the urge to convert to decimals too early. $\frac{1}{3}$ is cleaner and more accurate than $0{,}333$. Carry symbolic fractions through your algebra; convert to decimals only at the very last step for the numerical answer.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Berechnen Sie:
>
> a) $\dfrac{1}{4} + \dfrac{1}{6}$
> b) $\dfrac{2}{3} \cdot \dfrac{9}{4}$
> c) $\dfrac{1/R_1 + 1/R_2}{R_1 \cdot R_2}$ (vereinfachen)

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a)** Common denominator of 4 and 6 is 12:

$$\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{3}{12} + \frac{2}{12} = \frac{5}{12}$$

**b)**

$$\frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{9}{4} = \frac{2 \cdot 9}{3 \cdot 4} = \frac{18}{12}$$

Simplify: divide both by 6. $= \frac{3}{2}$.

**c)** Combine the top first: $\frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} = \frac{R_2 + R_1}{R_1 R_2}$.

Then divide by $R_1 R_2$: that's multiply by $\frac{1}{R_1 R_2}$:

$$\frac{R_2 + R_1}{R_1 R_2} \cdot \frac{1}{R_1 R_2} = \frac{R_1 + R_2}{R_1^2 R_2^2}$$

</details>

  difficulty="easy"
  type="short_derivation"
  question_de="Vereinfachen Sie 3/4 + 1/2."
  question_en="Simplify 3/4 + 1/2."
  correct_latex="\\frac{5}{4}"
  hint_en="Find common denominator (4). Rewrite 1/2 = 2/4, then add."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $\\dfrac{5}{4}$** (equivalently $1{,}25$).

$$\\frac{3}{4} + \\frac{1}{2} = \\frac{3}{4} + \\frac{2}{4} = \\frac{5}{4}$$

Common denominator is 4 (since 2 divides 4). $1/2 = 2/4$. Then add numerators.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Adding numerators and denominators: (3+1)/(4+2) = 4/6",
      why_en: "FRACTIONS ARE NOT ADDED BY ADDING NUMERATORS AND DENOMINATORS. This is the #1 fraction mistake. You MUST find a common denominator first. $\\frac{a}{b} + \\frac{c}{d} = \\frac{ad+bc}{bd}$ — note the $bd$ in denominator, not $b+d$."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why fractions add that way.**

Think of fractions as slices. $3/4$ is 3 slices of a 4-slice pizza. $1/2$ is 1 slice of a 2-slice pizza. If the slices aren't the same size, you can't just count them. Cutting the 2-slice pizza into 4 equally gives 2 slices of 4 — now $1/2$ matches $2/4$ in slice size. Now you can add: $3 + 2 = 5$ slices of 4-slice pizza, i.e. $5/4$ — more than one full pizza.

The common-denominator step is ALWAYS "make the slice sizes match".

**In engineering.** Parallel resistors: $\\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \\frac{1}{R_1} + \\frac{1}{R_2}$ — a classic fraction-addition application. Get it wrong and your circuit analysis is wrong.
  `}
/>

<Exercise
  id="track-0-bruch-l1-ex2"
  difficulty="easy"
  type="short_derivation"
  question_de="Dividieren Sie: (2/3) / (4/5)."
  question_en="Divide: (2/3) / (4/5)."
  correct_latex="\\frac{5}{6}"
  hint_en="Dividing by a fraction = multiplying by its reciprocal. Then simplify."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $\\dfrac{5}{6}$.**

$$\\frac{2/3}{4/5} = \\frac{2}{3} \\cdot \\frac{5}{4} = \\frac{2 \\cdot 5}{3 \\cdot 4} = \\frac{10}{12}$$

Simplify: divide by 2: $= \\dfrac{5}{6}$.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Multiplying instead of taking reciprocal: (2/3)(4/5) = 8/15",
      why_en: "DIVIDING by a fraction means MULTIPLYING by its reciprocal. $\\frac{a/b}{c/d} = \\frac{a}{b} \\cdot \\frac{d}{c}$. Multiplying numerators and denominators directly (which would be correct for simple multiplication) gives the wrong answer here."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why division flips the denominator.**

Division is the inverse of multiplication. $\\frac{a}{c/d}$ is the answer to "what do I multiply by $c/d$ to get $a$?" If you multiply $a$ by $d/c$, you get $\\frac{ad}{c}$. Multiplying that by $c/d$ gives back $\\frac{ad}{c} \\cdot \\frac{c}{d} = \\frac{adc}{cd} = a$. ✓ So $a \\div (c/d) = a \\cdot (d/c)$.

In shorthand: "invert and multiply" (or in German: *mit dem Kehrwert multiplizieren*).
  `}
/>

<Exercise
  id="track-0-bruch-l1-ex3"
  difficulty="medium"
  type="short_derivation"
  question_de="Vereinfachen Sie den Bruch 24 / 36."
  question_en="Simplify the fraction 24/36."
  correct_latex="\\frac{2}{3}"
  hint_en="Find the GCD of 24 and 36 and divide both by it. Or, factor and cancel."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $\\dfrac{2}{3}$.**

**Method: factor and cancel.**

$24 = 2 \\cdot 12 = 2 \\cdot 2 \\cdot 2 \\cdot 3 = 2^3 \\cdot 3$

$36 = 4 \\cdot 9 = 2^2 \\cdot 3^2$

Common factors: $2^2 \\cdot 3 = 12$. Cancel:

$$\\frac{24}{36} = \\frac{2 \\cdot 12}{3 \\cdot 12} = \\frac{2}{3}$$

**Alternative: find GCD step by step.** Both divisible by 2: $12/18$. Both divisible by 2 again: $6/9$. Both divisible by 3: $2/3$. Same answer via successive reduction.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Simplifying too early or incorrectly: 12/18 (half-reduced)",
      why_en: "12/18 is correct but not fully simplified. Both 12 and 18 share a factor of 6, giving 2/3. Always check whether further reduction is possible; the final answer should have NO common factor between numerator and denominator."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**Why bother simplifying.**

1. **Clarity.** $\\frac{2}{3}$ is obviously smaller than 1 and close to $0{,}667$. $\\frac{24}{36}$ requires mental arithmetic to recognize it.
2. **Further algebra.** If you'll combine this fraction with others, simpler form means smaller common denominators later.
3. **Klausur convention.** German exam graders expect simplified answers. "$\\frac{24}{36}$" is technically correct but marks may be deducted for not simplifying.

**In engineering.** Symbolic fractions often simplify dramatically. Compressor pressure-ratio formulas, heat-exchange resistance networks, differential equations' solution forms — all benefit from algebraic simplification before plugging in numbers.
  `}
/>

<Exercise
  id="track-0-bruch-l1-ex4"
  difficulty="klausur"
  type="short_derivation"
  question_de="Vereinfachen Sie: (1/a + 1/b) / (a + b)."
  question_en="Simplify: (1/a + 1/b) / (a + b)."
  correct_latex="\\frac{1}{ab}"
  hint_en="First combine the top into one fraction. Then divide."
  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $\\dfrac{1}{ab}$.**

**Step 1. Combine the top.**

$$\\frac{1}{a} + \\frac{1}{b} = \\frac{b + a}{ab}$$

**Step 2. Divide by $(a + b)$.**

$$\\frac{(a + b)/(ab)}{a + b} = \\frac{a + b}{ab} \\cdot \\frac{1}{a + b} = \\frac{1}{ab}$$

(The $a+b$ factors cancel.)
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Distributing wrong: 1/(a²+b) or similar",
      why_en: "The expression needs careful bracket-handling. $\\frac{1/a + 1/b}{a+b}$ is NOT the same as $\\frac{1}{a(a+b)} + \\frac{1}{b(a+b)}$ — wait, actually... let me recheck. $\\frac{1/a + 1/b}{a+b} = \\frac{1/a}{a+b} + \\frac{1/b}{a+b} = \\frac{1}{a(a+b)} + \\frac{1}{b(a+b)}$. These are all valid representations of the same thing. The simplified form $\\frac{1}{ab}$ requires combining the top first, then cancelling $(a+b)$."
    }
  ]}
  discussion_mdx={`
**In engineering — parallel resistance.**

Parallel resistor formula: $\\frac{1}{R_{parallel}} = \\frac{1}{R_1} + \\frac{1}{R_2}$. For two resistors:

$$R_{parallel} = \\frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Derivation: $\\frac{1}{R_{parallel}} = \\frac{R_1 + R_2}{R_1 R_2}$, so $R_{parallel} = \\frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$ (taking the reciprocal).

This is exactly the same fraction manipulation you just did, in disguise. The algebra you practiced on $\\frac{1/a + 1/b}{a+b}$ shows up verbatim in circuit analysis, parallel heat transfer, parallel flow in piping networks.
  `}
/>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** If a fraction can be simplified, simplify it — examiners notice. The four checks:

1. Can you cancel a common factor?
2. Can you combine two fractions into one?
3. Does an $(a+b)$ or similar appear in both numerator and denominator?
4. Is the final form "as clean as possible"?

Fractions left unsimplified lose 0.5–1 mark even when arithmetically correct.

</Callout>
