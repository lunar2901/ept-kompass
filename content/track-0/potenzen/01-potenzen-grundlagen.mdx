## Why this concept exists

A quarter of all engineering formulas have a power in them. Kinetic energy is $\tfrac{1}{2}m c^2$ (a square). Wind-turbine power is $\propto c^3$ (a cube). Heat radiation is $\propto T^4$ (Stefan-Boltzmann). When you want to manipulate these — solve for one variable, take a ratio, compare two cases — you need the rules of exponent arithmetic cold.

This lesson is a refresher, not new territory. If you remember that $x^a \cdot x^b = x^{a+b}$ you can skim; if you hesitate on that one, stay. By the end you will be comfortable with powers of any sign, with roots as fractional exponents, and with the scientific-notation arithmetic that every lab and Klausur requires.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Definition

$a^n$ (for positive integer $n$) means $a$ multiplied by itself $n$ times:

$$a^1 = a, \quad a^2 = a \cdot a, \quad a^3 = a \cdot a \cdot a, \quad \dots$$

Extensions to other exponents are defined so that the rules below stay valid:

- $a^0 = 1$ (for $a \neq 0$) — required for $a^m \cdot a^0 = a^m$ to hold.
- $a^{-n} = 1/a^n$ — required for $a^n \cdot a^{-n} = a^{n-n} = a^0 = 1$.
- $a^{1/2} = \sqrt{a}$ — required for $(a^{1/2})^2 = a^1 = a$.
- $a^{p/q} = \sqrt[q]{a^p}$ — rational exponents via combined root.

### The six rules

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Potenzgesetze"
  name_en="Power rules"
>

For any $a, b > 0$ and real $m, n$:

$$a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n} \qquad \text{(multiply → add exponents)}$$
$$\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n} \qquad \text{(divide → subtract)}$$
$$(a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n} \qquad \text{(power of a power → multiply)}$$
$$(a \cdot b)^n = a^n \cdot b^n$$
$$\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}$$
$$a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$$

</FormulaCard>

These are not separate facts — they are consequences of the definition of exponentiation. If you remember the first one ($a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$), the others can be derived on the fly.

### Rational exponents and roots

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Wurzeln als Potenzen"
  name_en="Roots as fractional powers"
  latex="\sqrt[n]{a} = a^{1/n}, \qquad \sqrt[n]{a^m} = a^{m/n}"
>

- $\sqrt{a} = a^{1/2}$
- $\sqrt[3]{a} = a^{1/3}$ (cube root)
- $a^{3/2} = (a^{1/2})^3 = (\sqrt{a})^3$

For negative bases with irrational exponents the definition needs care; for engineering formulas (positive bases like masses, volumes, temperatures), this isn't an issue.

</FormulaCard>

### Scientific notation — engineering's default number format

Numbers in engineering span enormous ranges: molecular masses $\approx 10^{-27}$ kg, solar power to Earth $\approx 10^{17}$ W. Scientific notation lets you manipulate these cleanly:

$$a \cdot 10^n$$

where $1 \leq a < 10$ (the "mantissa") and $n$ is an integer (the "order of magnitude").

**Multiplication:** multiply mantissas, add exponents.

$$(3 \cdot 10^4) \cdot (2 \cdot 10^3) = 6 \cdot 10^7$$

**Division:** divide mantissas, subtract exponents.

$$\frac{6 \cdot 10^7}{3 \cdot 10^4} = 2 \cdot 10^3$$

**Exponentiation:** exponentiate mantissa, multiply exponents.

$$(2 \cdot 10^3)^2 = 4 \cdot 10^6$$

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Notation in German engineering.** The decimal comma is standard in German texts: $6{,}022 \cdot 10^{23}$, not $6.022 \cdot 10^{23}$. Klausuren accept either; be consistent within one problem. In this app we default to comma in German prose, period in English walkthroughs.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Vereinfachen Sie:
>
> a) $\dfrac{a^7 \cdot a^{-3}}{a^2}$
> b) $(2 \cdot 10^{-3})^3$
> c) $\sqrt[3]{8 x^9}$

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Exponent arithmetic.**

Multiplying $a^7 \cdot a^{-3} = a^{7 + (-3)} = a^4$.
Dividing by $a^2$: $a^4 / a^2 = a^{4 - 2} = a^2$.

Result: $\boxed{a^2}$.

**b) Cubing a scientific-notation number.**

$(2 \cdot 10^{-3})^3 = 2^3 \cdot 10^{-3 \cdot 3} = 8 \cdot 10^{-9}$.

**c) Cube root of a product.**

$\sqrt[3]{8 x^9} = \sqrt[3]{8} \cdot \sqrt[3]{x^9} = 2 \cdot x^{9/3} = 2 x^3$.

</details>

**Answer: $1{,}8 \\cdot 10^7 = 18\\,000\\,000$.**

$$(3 \\cdot 10^4)^2 = 3^2 \\cdot (10^4)^2 = 9 \\cdot 10^8$$

**Step 2. Multiply by $2 \\cdot 10^{-2}$.**

Mantissas: $9 \\cdot 2 = 18$.

Product: $18 \\cdot 10^6 = 1{,}8 \\cdot 10^7 = 18\\,000\\,000$.

**The three rules of scientific-notation arithmetic (cheat sheet).**

Multiply $(a \\cdot 10^m) \\cdot (b \\cdot 10^n) = ab \\cdot 10^{m+n}$.

  solution_mdx={`
**Answer: $\\dfrac{b^6}{a^5}$ (equivalently $a^{-5} b^6$).**

**Step 1. Expand the bracket.**

$(a^3 \\cdot b^{-2})^{-1} = a^{-3} \\cdot b^{2}$

(applying $(xy)^n = x^n y^n$ with $n = -1$, and $(x^m)^n = x^{mn}$).

**Step 2. Multiply everything.**

$$(a^{-3} \\cdot b^2) \\cdot a^{-2} \\cdot b^4$$

Collect $a$'s: $a^{-3} \\cdot a^{-2} = a^{-5}$.
Collect $b$'s: $b^2 \\cdot b^4 = b^6$.

Result: $a^{-5} b^6 = \\dfrac{b^6}{a^5}$.
  `}
  common_mistakes={[
    {
      wrong: "Distributing wrong: (a³·b⁻²)⁻¹ = a⁻⁴·b⁻³",
      why_en: "The rule $(xy)^n = x^n y^n$ distributes the OUTER exponent to each factor. For $n = -1$ on each, you'd get $a^{-3}$ and $b^{2}$ — not $a^{-4}$.     },
    {
      wrong: "Adding exponents across different bases: treating a and b as one variable",
      why_en: "$a$ and $b$ are independent. Collect $a$'s with $a$'s and $b$'s with $b$'s, NEVER mix them. $a^2 \\cdot b^3 \\neq (ab)^5$ — those are fundamentally different expressions."
    }

  discussion_mdx={`
**Algebraic manipulation in engineering.**

This kind of exponent arithmetic appears when you rearrange formulas. Example: the Reynolds number $Re = \\rho c L / \\mu$ can be written as $Re = \\rho c L \\mu^{-1}$. If you want to express $L$ in terms of the others: $L = Re \\cdot \\rho^{-1} c^{-1} \\mu$. The algebra of negative exponents is how you fluidly move variables between numerator and denominator.

**Connection forward.** Dimensional analysis in mechanics and fluid flow is a giant exercise in exponent arithmetic. The Buckingham-π theorem derives dimensionless groups from products of powers of physical quantities; you get a system of linear equations in the exponents, solved with matrix algebra. The pattern is exactly what we just did in this exercise — collect, simplify, interpret.
  `}

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** When simplifying a messy algebraic expression with powers, work **one variable at a time**. Collect all $a$'s, then all $b$'s, then all $c$'s. This prevents mistakes from mixing bases. And always write the final answer with positive exponents (moving negative-exponent factors to the denominator) — German exam conventions prefer this.

</Callout>
