## Why this concept exists

Chemical reactions and process engineering balance the number of atoms / molecules, not grams. To think like an engineer in a plant, you translate masses into <GermanTerm de="Stoffmenge" article="die" en="amount of substance" /> (moles) using the molar mass, balance the equation at the molecular level, then translate back to kilograms for the process.

This lesson refreshes the three conversions that tie everything together: mass ↔ moles ↔ molecules, and the role of the molar mass as the bridge.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Mole — the chemist's unit

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Avogadro-Konstante und Mol-Definition"
  name_en="Avogadro constant and mole definition"
  latex="N_A = 6{,}022 \cdot 10^{23}\,\mathrm{mol^{-1}}"
>

One mole of a substance contains $N_A$ particles (atoms, molecules, ions — whichever the substance's unit is). So:

- 1 mol of water contains $6{,}022 \cdot 10^{23}$ H₂O molecules.
- 1 mol of iron contains $6{,}022 \cdot 10^{23}$ Fe atoms.
- 1 mol of electrons contains $6{,}022 \cdot 10^{23}$ electrons.

Choosing the right "particle type" is important. The mole is a unit of counting, not of mass or volume.

</FormulaCard>

### Molar mass — grams per mole

The molar mass $M$ of a substance is the mass of 1 mol of it. Units: g/mol (or equivalently kg/kmol, kg·mol⁻¹).

Key numbers to memorise for combustion and process engineering:

| Substance | $M$ (g/mol) |
| --- | --- |
| H (atomic hydrogen) | 1.008 |
| C | 12.01 |
| N | 14.01 |
| O | 16.00 |
| Air (average) | 28.96 |
| H₂O | 18.02 |
| N₂ | 28.01 |
| O₂ | 32.00 |
| CO₂ | 44.01 |
| CH₄ | 16.04 |

For compounds, sum atomic masses: $M(\mathrm{H_2O}) = 2 \cdot 1{,}008 + 16{,}00 \approx 18{,}02\,\mathrm{g/mol}$.

### The three-way conversion

  name_de="Mass ↔ Mol ↔ Teilchen"
  name_en="Mass ↔ moles ↔ particles"
  latex="n = \frac{m}{M}, \qquad N = n \cdot N_A"
  variables={[

    { symbol: "M", name_de: "molare Masse", name_en: "molar mass", unit: "g/mol or kg/mol" },
    { symbol: "n", name_de: "Stoffmenge", name_en: "amount of substance", unit: "mol" },
    { symbol: "N", name_de: "Teilchenzahl", name_en: "number of particles", unit: "—" },

>

To go mass → moles: divide by $M$. Moles → particles: multiply by $N_A$.

</FormulaCard>

### Balancing a simple reaction

Methane combustion: $\mathrm{CH_4} + 2\,\mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow \mathrm{CO_2} + 2\,\mathrm{H_2O}$.

Read as: 1 mol of methane reacts with 2 mol of oxygen to produce 1 mol of CO₂ and 2 mol of water.

Cross-check atomic balance:
- C: 1 in, 1 out ✓
- H: 4 in, 4 out ✓
- O: 4 in (2·O₂), 4 out (2 in CO₂, 2 in H₂O) ✓

The *stoichiometric coefficients* (1, 2, 1, 2) tell you the MOLE ratios — not mass ratios.

### Mass of products from mass of reactants

Example: burn 10 kg of methane completely. How much CO₂ is produced?

1. Moles of methane: $n_{\mathrm{CH_4}} = 10\,000\,\mathrm{g} / 16{,}04\,\mathrm{g/mol} \approx 624\,\mathrm{mol}$.
2. Moles of CO₂ produced: 1 mol of CH₄ → 1 mol of CO₂, so $n_{\mathrm{CO_2}} \approx 624\,\mathrm{mol}$.
3. Mass of CO₂: $m = n \cdot M = 624 \cdot 44{,}01 \approx 27\,500\,\mathrm{g} = 27{,}5\,\mathrm{kg}$.

So 10 kg of methane produces about 27.5 kg of CO₂. This factor (~2.75 kg CO₂ per kg CH₄) is often quoted directly in environmental engineering.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Wie viel Gramm Wasser werden freigesetzt, wenn 44 g CO₂ entstehen (aus vollständiger Methanverbrennung)?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

Reaction: $\mathrm{CH_4} + 2\,\mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow \mathrm{CO_2} + 2\,\mathrm{H_2O}$.

Mole ratio CO₂ : H₂O = 1 : 2. For every mole of CO₂, two moles of water.

**Step 1. Mol CO₂.** $n(\mathrm{CO_2}) = 44 / 44{,}01 \approx 1{,}00\,\mathrm{mol}$.

**Step 2. Mol H₂O.** $n(\mathrm{H_2O}) = 2 \cdot 1{,}00 = 2{,}00\,\mathrm{mol}$.

**Step 3. Mass H₂O.** $m = n \cdot M = 2 \cdot 18{,}02 \approx 36\,\mathrm{g}$.

So **36 g** of water are released.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For stoichiometry problems, always:

1. Write and balance the equation.
2. Identify the mole ratio between the known substance and the wanted one.
3. Convert mass → mol (divide by M), apply ratio, convert mol → mass (multiply by M).

Most errors come from skipping step 1 or forgetting to use the mole ratio (steps 2-3 cancel out cleanly if the ratio is 1:1).

</Callout>
