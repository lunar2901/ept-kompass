## Why this concept exists

Before thermodynamics, before fluid dynamics, before anything — there is $F = ma$. Newton's three laws are the backbone of every mechanical-engineering calculation: static forces on beams, rockets accelerating, fluids flowing, gears meshing. This lesson refreshes the three laws and the energy-momentum language that grows out of them.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The three laws

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Newton's Gesetze"
  name_en="Newton's laws"
>

**1. Trägheitsgesetz (Law of inertia).** A body not acted on by any net force moves with constant velocity (possibly zero). Ohne äußere Kraft keine Änderung der Bewegung.

**2. Aktionsprinzip (Action law).**

$$\vec{F}_{net} = m \, \vec{a}$$

Net force equals mass times acceleration. Vector equation in three components.

**3. Reaktionsprinzip (Reaction law).** For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction: $\vec{F}_{A \to B} = -\vec{F}_{B \to A}$.

</FormulaCard>

### Derivatives and integrals

Kinematic relations you'll use:

- $\vec{v} = \dfrac{\mathrm{d}\vec{s}}{\mathrm{d}t}$ — velocity is derivative of position.
- $\vec{a} = \dfrac{\mathrm{d}\vec{v}}{\mathrm{d}t}$ — acceleration is derivative of velocity.
- Integrating: $\vec{v}(t) = \vec{v}_0 + \int \vec{a}\,\mathrm{d}t$, $\vec{s}(t) = \vec{s}_0 + \int \vec{v}\,\mathrm{d}t$.

For constant acceleration (free fall with $g$, constant-thrust rocket):

$$v = v_0 + a\,t, \qquad s = s_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2} a t^2$$

### Energy and momentum

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Kinetische Energie und Impuls"
  name_en="Kinetic energy and momentum"
  latex="E_{kin} = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2, \qquad \vec{p} = m \vec{v}"
>

Kinetic energy is a scalar (J), momentum is a vector (kg·m/s).

**Work-energy theorem:** the net work done on a body equals its change in kinetic energy:

$$W_{net} = \int \vec{F}_{net} \cdot \mathrm{d}\vec{s} = \Delta E_{kin}$$

**Momentum conservation (closed system):** total momentum is conserved in the absence of external forces:

$$\sum \vec{p}_{vor} = \sum \vec{p}_{nach}$$

The backbone of every collision and every rocket calculation.

</FormulaCard>

### Potential energy

For conservative forces (gravity, spring, electrostatic), define a potential energy $E_{pot}$ such that $\vec{F} = -\nabla E_{pot}$:

- Gravitational (uniform near Earth's surface): $E_{pot} = m g h$.
- Spring (Hooke): $E_{pot} = \tfrac{1}{2} k x^2$.

**Energy conservation** in conservative systems: $E_{kin} + E_{pot} = \mathrm{const}$.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Ein Ball der Masse $m = 0{,}5\,\mathrm{kg}$ fällt aus $h = 20\,\mathrm{m}$ Höhe. Berechnen Sie die Aufprallgeschwindigkeit (ohne Luftwiderstand, $g = 9{,}81\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$).

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Method: energy conservation.**

Initial: $E_{kin,0} = 0$, $E_{pot,0} = m g h = 0{,}5 \cdot 9{,}81 \cdot 20 = 98{,}1\,\mathrm{J}$.

Final (just before impact): $E_{pot,f} = 0$, so $E_{kin,f} = 98{,}1\,\mathrm{J}$.

$\tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = 98{,}1 \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{2 \cdot 98{,}1 / 0{,}5} = \sqrt{392{,}4} \approx 19{,}8\,\mathrm{m/s}$.

**Alternative: kinematics.** $v = \sqrt{2 g h} = \sqrt{2 \cdot 9{,}81 \cdot 20} \approx 19{,}8\,\mathrm{m/s}$. Same answer.

Either method works; energy conservation is usually faster.

</details>

The force to stretch a spring is $F = k x$ — linear in displacement. The work to stretch further is force × distance, integrated:

**Elastic vs inelastic collisions.**

All isolated-system collisions conserve momentum. They differ in what happens to kinetic energy:

- **Elastic**: KE conserved (idealised billiard balls, some atomic collisions).
- **Inelastic**: KE partially converted to heat, sound, deformation (cars crashing, clay balls sticking).
- **Perfectly inelastic**: maximum KE loss — the two bodies stick together post-collision.

Rocket propulsion is the same physics: total momentum of rocket + ejected exhaust is conserved; the rocket speeds up because the exhaust is thrown backward.

**Connection to engineering.**
- Car crash test analysis: compute momentum before, momentum after — difference (if any) came from external forces (friction, crash structure).
- Fluid momentum: used in pump calculations, jet thrust (rockets, jet engines).
- Rigid-body dynamics: angular momentum conservation governs rotating machines, gyroscopes, flywheels.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For mechanics problems, ask three questions at the start:

1. **System boundaries?** What am I tracking — single body, multi-body, fluid volume?
2. **External forces?** If zero, momentum is conserved. If some forces are conservative, total mechanical energy is conserved if no dissipation.
3. **Time or position dependence?** If given forces as a function of position, use work-energy. If given as a function of time, use impulse-momentum.

Choosing the right approach is usually more important than the arithmetic.

</Callout>
