## Why this concept exists

The ideal-gas model from the last lesson assumes point particles with no interactions. At moderate conditions that is astonishingly accurate (< 1 % error for air at 1 bar). But push a gas to high pressure, or cool it toward its dew point, and the two assumptions break: molecular volume becomes comparable to the gas volume, and intermolecular attractions become comparable to kinetic energy.

This lesson introduces two tools for real-gas deviations: the <GermanTerm de="van-der-Waals-Gleichung" article="die" en="van der Waals equation" />, and the <GermanTerm de="Realgasfaktor" article="der" en="compressibility factor" /> $Z$. Both live at the interface between the pristine ideal model and the messy tabulated real data you will use in Thermodynamik II.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The two corrections

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Van-der-Waals-Gleichung"
  name_en="Van der Waals equation"
  latex="\left(p + \frac{a\,n^2}{V^2}\right)(V - n\,b) = n\,R\,T"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "a", name_de: "Anziehungsparameter", name_en: "attraction parameter", unit: "Pa·m⁶/mol²" },
    { symbol: "b", name_de: "Eigenvolumen-Parameter", name_en: "excluded-volume parameter", unit: "m³/mol" },
    { symbol: "n", name_de: "Stoffmenge", name_en: "amount of substance", unit: "mol" },
  ]}
>

Two corrections to $pV = nRT$:

- The $+a\,n^2/V^2$ term *adds* a correction to the measured pressure to compensate for attractive forces between molecules that reduce the wall-impact pressure.
- The $-n\,b$ term *subtracts* the volume the molecules themselves take up (finite size), since that space is unavailable to free motion.

Both $a$ and $b$ are gas-specific constants, tabulated. For air: $a \approx 0{,}136\,\mathrm{Pa\,m^6/mol^2}$, $b \approx 3{,}65 \times 10^{-5}\,\mathrm{m^3/mol}$. For water: $a \approx 0{,}554$, $b \approx 3{,}05 \times 10^{-5}$ — reflecting water's stronger intermolecular attractions.

</FormulaCard>

### The compressibility factor $Z$

Easier to use in engineering is the <GermanTerm de="Realgasfaktor" article="der" en="compressibility factor" /> $Z$:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Realgasfaktor"
  name_en="Compressibility factor"
  latex="Z = \frac{p\,v}{R_s\,T}"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "Z", name_de: "Realgasfaktor", name_en: "compressibility factor", unit: "—" },
    { symbol: "p", name_de: "Druck", name_en: "pressure", unit: "Pa" },
    { symbol: "v", name_de: "spezifisches Volumen", name_en: "specific volume", unit: "m³/kg" },
    { symbol: "R_s", name_de: "spezifische Gaskonstante", name_en: "specific gas constant", unit: "J/(kg·K)" },
    { symbol: "T", name_de: "Temperatur", name_en: "temperature", unit: "K" },
  ]}
>

- Ideal gas: $Z = 1$ exactly.
- Most real gases at low $p$ and high $T$: $Z \approx 1$ within a few %.
- Real gases at high $p$: $Z$ can be as low as 0.3 (attractive forces dominate, gas more compressible than ideal) or as high as 2–5 (excluded volume dominates, gas less compressible than ideal).

$Z$ is tabulated or charted as a function of reduced pressure $p_r = p/p_c$ and reduced temperature $T_r = T/T_c$, where $p_c$ and $T_c$ are the gas's critical-point values. This is the **principle of corresponding states** — most real gases at the same $p_r$, $T_r$ have nearly the same $Z$.

</FormulaCard>

### When to reach for tables instead of formulas

For water, refrigerants, and many hydrocarbons, engineers use **property tables** (*Wasserdampftafel*, steam tables) directly — the real-gas equation is too approximate for power-plant calculations. A typical steam-table entry looks like:

> $p = 10\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T = 400\,\mathrm{°C}$: $v = 0{,}3066\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$, $h = 3264\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$, $s = 7{,}465\,\mathrm{kJ/(kg\,K)}$

That is the real-gas answer, measured to four-figure accuracy. No formula can compete in that regime. The ideal-gas calculation would give $v_{\mathrm{id}} = R_s T / p = 461 \cdot 673 / 10^6 = 0{,}310\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$ — off by 1 % in this case, acceptable for some uses but not for precise cycle analysis.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** If the Klausur names a *specific* substance (water, R-134a, ammonia) and gives $p$ and $T$ near a phase boundary, expect to use the table. If it says *"air" or "gas"* and conditions are moderate ($p < 10\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T$ well above dew point), use the ideal-gas law. When unsure, write down both the ideal estimate and a short sentence saying whether you need a real-gas correction.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Der Realgasfaktor von Luft bei $p = 50\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ beträgt $Z = 0{,}985$. Das spezifische Volumen der Luft in diesem Zustand ist zu bestimmen.
>
> *a)* Schätzen Sie $v$ mit der idealen Gasgleichung.
> *b)* Korrigieren Sie Ihre Schätzung mit dem Realgasfaktor.
> *c)* Wie groß ist die relative Abweichung?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Ideal-gas estimate.**

$$v_{\mathrm{id}} = \frac{R_s T}{p} = \frac{287 \cdot 300}{50 \cdot 10^5} = 0{,}01722\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$$

**b) Real-gas correction via $Z$.**

$$Z = \frac{pv}{R_s T} \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_{\mathrm{real}} = Z \cdot \frac{R_s T}{p} = Z \cdot v_{\mathrm{id}}$$

$$v_{\mathrm{real}} = 0{,}985 \cdot 0{,}01722 = 0{,}01696\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$$

**c) Deviation.**

$$\frac{v_{\mathrm{id}} - v_{\mathrm{real}}}{v_{\mathrm{real}}} = \frac{0{,}01722 - 0{,}01696}{0{,}01696} \approx 1{,}5\,\%$$

At 50 bar and 300 K the ideal estimate is 1.5 % high — small, but not zero. For tight power-plant heat-rate calculations this matters; for estimating a compressed-air tank volume it would usually be ignored.

</details>
