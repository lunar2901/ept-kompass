<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this concept exists

You now know what a *System* is — everything inside the box you drew. The next question is **how do we describe what is inside the box?** We use numbers: temperature, pressure, mass, volume, internal energy. These numbers are <GermanTerm de="Zustandsgrößen" article="die" plural="Zustandsgrößen" en="state variables" />.

But there are two species of state variable, and mixing them up is the second-most common way students lose marks on Klausuren (the first being drawing the *Systemgrenze* in the wrong place). Every state variable is either **intensive** or **extensive**, and the rules for combining them are different.

Intuition: cut your system in half. The quantities that stay the same in each half are **intensive**; the quantities that also get cut in half are **extensive**.

</Callout>

## Theorie — the formal picture

### Definition by the cut-in-half test

Consider a homogeneous system at equilibrium and imagine cutting it into two equal halves. Each half is itself a valid thermodynamic system. Now compare a property of the whole to the same property of one half:

| Property | Whole system | Each half | Classification |
| --- | :---: | :---: | --- |
| Temperature $T$ | $T$ | $T$ | **intensive** |
| Pressure $p$ | $p$ | $p$ | **intensive** |
| Density $\rho$ | $\rho$ | $\rho$ | **intensive** |
| Mass $m$ | $m$ | $m/2$ | **extensive** |
| Volume $V$ | $V$ | $V/2$ | **extensive** |
| Internal energy $U$ | $U$ | $U/2$ | **extensive** |
| Entropy $S$ | $S$ | $S/2$ | **extensive** |

The test is: *if you double the amount of matter in the system at constant T and p, does the quantity double too?* If yes, extensive. If no, intensive.

### The common engineering list

>

**Intensive** (do not depend on size):
- Temperatur $T$ (in Kelvin)
- Druck $p$ (in Pascal)
- Dichte $\rho$ (in kg/m³)
- spezifisches Volumen $v = 1/\rho$ (in m³/kg)
- Konzentration, Stoffmengenanteil
- chemisches Potenzial $\mu_i$

**Extensive** (scale with size):
- Masse $m$ (in kg)
- Stoffmenge $n$ (in mol)
- Volumen $V$ (in m³)
- innere Energie $U$ (in J)
- Enthalpie $H = U + pV$ (in J)
- Entropie $S$ (in J/K)

</FormulaCard>

### The trick: extensive divided by extensive → intensive

This is the single most useful manipulation you will do. Take any extensive quantity and divide by the mass or the moles in the system. The result is intensive, and we call it a **spezifische Größe** (specific quantity, per unit mass) or **molare Größe** (molar quantity, per mole).

>

The lower-case symbols $v$, $u$, $h$, $s$ are the convention in thermodynamics. Steam tables are almost always printed in specific-quantity form, so that a single entry *"$h_1 = 3275\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$"* works regardless of whether your process handles 1 g or 1000 kg of steam — you just multiply at the end by $m$ or $\dot{m}$.

</FormulaCard>

### A subtlety: density is intensive even though it involves extensive quantities

Density is mass over volume: $\rho = m/V$. Both $m$ and $V$ are extensive. But when you cut the system in half, *both* get cut in half, so their ratio stays the same. This is the general rule:

> The ratio of two extensive quantities, taken over the **same** system, is intensive.

<Callout variant="note">

A sign that a quantity is intensive: its units contain a denominator with a mass unit, volume unit, or mole unit — *per kilogram*, *per litre*, *per mole*. Units like J/(kg·K) = *Joule pro Kilogramm Kelvin* are always intensive.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe — worked example in Klausur style

> **Aufgabe.** Ein Gasbehälter enthält 4 kg Stickstoff bei $T = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ und $p = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$. Das Volumen beträgt $V = 1{,}78\,\mathrm{m^3}$.
>
> *a)* Welche der angegebenen Größen sind intensiv, welche extensiv?
> *b)* Berechnen Sie das spezifische Volumen $v$ und die Dichte $\rho$.
> *c)* Der Behälter wird mit einer Trennwand in zwei gleiche Hälften geteilt. Welche Werte haben $T$, $p$, $m$, $V$, $v$ und $\rho$ in jeder Hälfte?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg — step by step (English)**</summary>

**Part a) Classification.**

- $T = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ — intensive (temperature).
- $p = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$ — intensive (pressure).
- $m = 4\,\mathrm{kg}$ — extensive (mass).
- $V = 1{,}78\,\mathrm{m^3}$ — extensive (volume).

**Part b) Specific volume and density.**

Specific volume:
$$v = \frac{V}{m} = \frac{1{,}78\,\mathrm{m^3}}{4\,\mathrm{kg}} = 0{,}445\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$$

Density is the reciprocal:
$$\rho = \frac{1}{v} = \frac{m}{V} = \frac{4\,\mathrm{kg}}{1{,}78\,\mathrm{m^3}} \approx 2{,}25\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}$$

Both $v$ and $\rho$ are intensive — as expected, they are ratios of extensive quantities taken over the same system.

**Part c) After halving with a partition.**

The partition creates two subsystems, each containing half the gas at the same $T$ and $p$:

| Quantity | Type | Whole | Each half |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| $T$ | intensive | $300\,\mathrm{K}$ | $300\,\mathrm{K}$ |
| $p$ | intensive | $2\,\mathrm{bar}$ | $2\,\mathrm{bar}$ |
| $m$ | extensive | $4\,\mathrm{kg}$ | $2\,\mathrm{kg}$ |
| $V$ | extensive | $1{,}78\,\mathrm{m^3}$ | $0{,}89\,\mathrm{m^3}$ |
| $v$ | intensive | $0{,}445\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$ | $0{,}445\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$ |
| $\rho$ | intensive | $2{,}25\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}$ | $2{,}25\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}$ |

Exactly what the definition predicts.

</details>

**Enthalpy flow: the workhorse of open-system thermodynamics.**

Every open-system energy balance (turbines, pumps, heat exchangers, compressors, throttles) has the term $\\dot{m}_i\\,h_i$ for each stream crossing the boundary. The general steady-state balance is:

$$
\\dot{Q} + P = \\sum_{\\text{out}} \\dot{m}\\,h \\; - \\; \\sum_{\\text{in}} \\dot{m}\\,h
$$

(plus kinetic- and potential-energy terms, usually negligible).

You will spend the majority of Thermodynamik I Kapitel 2 computing $h$ values from tables and doing this bookkeeping. Being comfortable with the unit game *specific × mass-flow = extensive-flow* now saves you confusion later.

**Variation — what if two streams enter and one leaves?**

A mixing tee has two inlet streams (1 and 2) and one outlet (3). Steady-state mass balance: $\\dot{m}_1 + \\dot{m}_2 = \\dot{m}_3$. Adiabatic energy balance (no $\\dot{Q}$, no $P$):

$$
\\dot{m}_1\\,h_1 + \\dot{m}_2\\,h_2 = \\dot{m}_3\\,h_3
$$

Solve for the mixed specific enthalpy $h_3$. This is the algebra of every building's hot-water loop.

Because a stream crossing an open-system boundary carries not only internal energy but also the work $pv$ it does pushing against whatever is upstream (the so-called *Verschiebearbeit*, flow work). Enthalpy $h = u + pv$ bundles these, and you do not have to track them separately. This is the single biggest practical reason engineering thermodynamics lives and breathes with $h$, not $u$, as soon as flows are involved.

**The steam-table habit.**

Every printed thermodynamic property table for water — Wasserdampftafel — lists properties in specific-quantity form: $v$ in m³/kg, $u$ in kJ/kg, $h$ in kJ/kg, $s$ in kJ/(kg·K). 1. Is it uppercase or lowercase? ($S$ or $s$, $H$ or $h$.) That tells you extensive or intensive.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** Before you start any Klausur calculation, glance at every symbol in the problem statement and sort them into two piles:

- capital letters with units like J, m³, kg, kJ/K → **extensive**;
- lower-case letters with units like J/kg, m³/kg, kJ/(kg·K), or "naked" T, p, ρ → **intensive**.

When you write your balance equation, every term on the left must match every term on the right in extensive-ness. If one side is per-kg and the other is total, you forgot a mass somewhere. This five-second sort catches half of all Klausur arithmetic errors.

</Callout>
