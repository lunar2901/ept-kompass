## Why this concept exists

Before we can talk about energy, heat, work, or any of the big ideas of thermodynamics, we have to draw a box around the thing we want to study. That box is called a **<GermanTerm de="System" article="das" plural="Systeme" en="system" />**. Everything outside the box is the **<GermanTerm de="Umgebung" article="die" plural="Umgebungen" en="surroundings" />**. The line between them is the **<GermanTerm de="Systemgrenze" article="die" plural="Systemgrenzen" en="system boundary" />**.

This sounds trivial, but it is the single most common place students lose marks in a TU Berlin Klausur. If you draw your *Systemgrenze* in the wrong place, every energy balance you write after that is wrong — even if your maths is perfect.

Think of it like accounting. Before you can say whether a company made a profit, you have to decide: *which* company? Where does its activity start and stop? Thermodynamics is the same. The *Systemgrenze* is where you count energy crossing in or out.

</Callout>

## Theorie — the formal picture

### Three kinds of system

Thermodynamics classifies every system by **what is allowed to cross its boundary**: mass, energy, both, or neither.

| Deutsch | English | Mass across boundary? | Energy across boundary? | Typical example |
| --- | --- | :---: | :---: | --- |
| **geschlossenes System** | closed system | ❌ no | ✅ yes | gas in a sealed piston-cylinder |
| **offenes System** | open system | ✅ yes | ✅ yes | steam turbine, pump, heat exchanger |
| **abgeschlossenes System** | isolated system | ❌ no | ❌ no | idealised thermos flask |

A subtlety that shows up on Klausuren: in the German engineering tradition an open system is often called a **<GermanTerm de="Kontrollraum" article="der" plural="Kontrollräume" en="control volume" />** and its boundary is the **<GermanTerm de="Kontrollfläche" article="die" plural="Kontrollflächen" en="control surface" />**. These are the same idea as *offenes System / Systemgrenze* but phrased in the language of flow processes. When you see *Kontrollraum* in an exam question, you are being asked to do an open-system balance.

### The boundary itself has properties

A *Systemgrenze* is not just a line — it has physical properties we assign:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Eigenschaften der Systemgrenze"
  name_en="Properties of the system boundary"
>
  A boundary can independently be:
  - **starr** (rigid) — volume cannot change, so no boundary work  
    or **beweglich** (movable) — piston can move, boundary work is possible
  - **adiabat** (adiabatic) — no heat crosses  
    or **diatherm** (diathermal) — heat can cross
  - **stofflich durchlässig** (mass-permeable)  
    or **stofflich undurchlässig** (mass-impermeable)
</FormulaCard>

A typical Klausur sentence is *"Das System sei durch eine starre, adiabate und stofflich undurchlässige Wand begrenzt."* — translated: the system has a rigid, adiabatic, mass-impermeable boundary. You should immediately conclude: no work, no heat, no mass flow. In other words, isolated.

### Drawing the boundary — what examiners actually check

When you answer an exam question you should:

1. **Draw the physical setup** — the piston, the tank, the pipe.
2. **Draw the *Systemgrenze* as a dashed line.** Every Klausur solution at TU Berlin uses a dashed line for this.
3. **Label all crossings** — every arrow is either a mass flow (<GermanTerm de="Massenstrom" article="der" plural="Massenströme" en="mass flow rate" />, symbol $\dot{m}$), a heat flow (<GermanTerm de="Wärmestrom" article="der" plural="Wärmeströme" en="heat flow rate" />, symbol $\dot{Q}$), or a power (<GermanTerm de="Leistung" article="die" plural="Leistungen" en="power" />, symbol $P$ or $\dot{W}$).
4. **State which type of system** it is in words: *"Das ist ein offenes, stationäres System."*

That last step — stating it in words, in German — is worth marks on almost every energy-balance problem.

## Musteraufgabe — worked example in Klausur style

> **Aufgabe.** Ein ideal isolierter, starrer Behälter enthält 2 kg Luft. Durch ein Rührwerk wird Arbeit in den Behälter eingebracht. Die Behälterwände sind wärmeundurchlässig und die Luft kann nicht entweichen.
>
> *a)* Um welchen Systemtyp handelt es sich? Begründen Sie.  
> *b)* Zeichnen Sie das System mit Systemgrenze und tragen Sie alle Energieflüsse ein.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg — step by step (English)**</summary>

**Step 1. Check what can cross the boundary.**

- *Masse?* The problem says *"die Luft kann nicht entweichen"* (the air cannot escape) → no mass crosses.
- *Wärme?* The walls are *"wärmeundurchlässig"* (adiabatic) → no heat crosses.
- *Arbeit?* A *Rührwerk* (stirrer) is doing work on the gas. Work is energy, and energy crosses the boundary through the shaft of the stirrer.

**Step 2. Classify.**

Mass: no. Heat: no. Energy in another form (work): yes.

This is a **geschlossenes, adiabates System**. It is **not** abgeschlossen, because energy is still being transferred (as shaft work). A common trap: students see *"isoliert"* in the question and write *abgeschlossen*. But *isoliert* here only means *thermally* isolated — the stirrer shaft still carries energy in.

**Step 3. German answer sentence for part a).**

*"Es handelt sich um ein geschlossenes, adiabates System, da weder Masse noch Wärme die Systemgrenze überschreiten, jedoch Arbeit über die Welle des Rührwerks zugeführt wird."*

**Step 4. Sketch for part b).**

Draw the tank. Draw a dashed line just inside the wall. Draw an arrow labelled $W_{\text{Welle}}$ (*Wellenarbeit*, shaft work) crossing the boundary pointing inward. No $\dot{Q}$ arrow. No $\dot{m}$ arrow. Write on the sketch: *geschlossenes, adiabates System*.

</details>

**Why this classification matters for what you compute next.**

Once you have said "offenes System", you commit to the open-system energy balance (the *Energiebilanz für stationäre Fließprozesse*):

$$
\\dot{Q} + P = \\dot{m} \\left[ (h_2 - h_1) + \\tfrac{1}{2}(c_2^2 - c_1^2) + g(z_2 - z_1) \\right]
$$

If you had classified it as closed, you would instead try to use $Q + W = \\Delta U$, which uses *internal energy* $U$, not *enthalpy* $H$. **That is the single biggest trap in the whole first-law chapter.** The reason open systems use enthalpy $h = u + pv$ is that mass crossing the boundary carries not only its internal energy but also does *Verschiebearbeit* (flow work, $pv$) against the pressure on the other side. **Correct answer: (c) abgeschlossenes System (ideal).**

**Reasoning.** Let's check each boundary property:

- *Mass?* Lid firmly closed → no mass crosses → **closed at minimum**.
- *Heat?* "Gut gedämmt" (well-insulated) and "bleibt fast heiß" (stays almost hot) → we idealise heat loss as zero → **adiabatic**.

## Was kommt als Nächstes

In the next lesson — **Zustandsgrößen: intensiv und extensiv** — we build on this picture. Once you know where the system ends, you can start describing *what is inside it*: temperature, pressure, volume, mass. These are *Zustandsgrößen*, and the distinction between **intensive** (temperature, pressure — do not depend on size) and **extensive** (volume, mass, internal energy — scale with size) is the second most common thing Klausuren test after boundary-drawing.
