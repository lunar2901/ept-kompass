<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this concept exists

You now know that the state of a system is described by a handful of <GermanTerm de="Zustandsgrößen" article="die" plural="Zustandsgrößen" en="state variables" />. A natural next question: **how many do I need to nail down to fully describe the state?**

The beautiful and slightly miraculous answer for the systems in this course is: **two, usually**. For a single-phase, pure substance at equilibrium, two intensive state variables are enough to fix every other one. This is the <GermanTerm de="Gibbssche Phasenregel" article="die" en="Gibbs phase rule" /> applied to the simplest case, and it is why thermodynamic diagrams are always 2D — a $(p, v)$ plot, a $(T, s)$ plot.

If two numbers fix a state, a state becomes a **point** in a 2D plane. And a **process** — the system moving from one state to another — becomes a **curve** from one point to another in that plane. That picture is the whole visual grammar of thermodynamics.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Two intensive variables fix a pure-substance state

For a single-phase pure substance at equilibrium (no chemical reactions, one component), you can pick any two *independent* intensive properties — $p$ and $T$, or $T$ and $v$, or $p$ and $s$ — and every other property follows. This is why steam tables are organised as a 2D lookup: you enter with $(p, T)$ or $(p, h)$ and read the rest.

At a two-phase boundary (boiling water, condensing steam) this rule tightens: only *one* intensive property is free, because $p$ and $T$ are no longer independent (they are linked by the Clausius-Clapeyron curve). You need one intensive property *plus* an extensive quality variable — the vapour fraction $x$.

<Callout variant="note">

**Gibbs phase rule** (general case):
$$F = C - P + 2$$
where $F$ = degrees of freedom (intensive), $C$ = number of chemical components, $P$ = number of coexisting phases. For a single pure substance ($C = 1$): one phase → $F = 2$; two phases in equilibrium → $F = 1$; the triple point → $F = 0$.

</Callout>

### State vs process, equation vs path

A **state** is a single frozen snapshot of the system. A **process** (*Prozess*, *Zustandsänderung*) is the *path* the system follows between two states. State variables have values *at a state*; process quantities like heat $Q$ and work $W$ only acquire values *over a path*.

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Zustandsgrößen vs. Prozessgrößen"
  name_en="State vs. process quantities"
>

**State quantities** (Zustandsgrößen): $T$, $p$, $v$, $u$, $h$, $s$. Exist at a single state. Their change from state 1 to state 2 depends only on the endpoints: $\Delta u = u_2 - u_1$.

**Process quantities** (Prozessgrößen): $q$, $w$. Exist only while the process is happening. Their value depends on the path, not just the endpoints: $q = \int \delta q$, not $\int du$.

Notation: use the exact differential $\mathrm{d}$ for state quantities ($\mathrm{d}u$, $\mathrm{d}h$), the inexact differential $\delta$ for process quantities ($\delta q$, $\delta w$).

</FormulaCard>

### Named processes you need to recognise

A single lecture contains a jargon cluster of *iso-*words. They all mean "at constant X":

| Prefix | Constant | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **iso**therm | temperature $T$ | gas expanding slowly in contact with a heat bath |
| **iso**bar | pressure $p$ | open beaker heated on a hotplate |
| **iso**chor | volume $V$ | sealed rigid tank heated |
| **iso**entrop | entropy $s$ | ideal turbine, ideal compressor |
| adiabat | $Q = 0$ | insulated process, any speed |
| polytrop | $pV^n = \mathrm{const}$ | real compressor (n = 1.2, say) |

**Important distinction.** *Isentrop* means "at constant entropy", which is stronger than "adiabat". An adiabatic process has $\delta Q = 0$ but can still have entropy rise (if irreversible). Isentropic = adiabatic + reversible.

### Quasi-static and reversible

Real processes proceed at finite speed. Near every piston there are pressure gradients, temperature gradients, tiny fluid accelerations — the gas is NOT in a single equilibrium state during the process. For the equations we want to write, we need to idealise:

- A <GermanTerm de="quasistatische Zustandsänderung" article="die" en="quasi-static change of state" /> is one that proceeds through a sequence of equilibrium states — slow enough that the gas is "uniform" at every instant.
- A **reversible** process is quasi-static AND dissipation-free (no friction, no heat across finite $\Delta T$). It can be run backward recovering the same states in reverse order.

Only reversible quasi-static processes trace out a well-defined *curve* on a $(p, v)$ diagram. A real irreversible process has no well-defined intermediate states, so the path is undrawable — only the endpoints are well-defined points.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

On a $(p, v)$ diagram, any real irreversible process is drawn as a **dashed curve** or just a pair of endpoints with a wavy arrow — emphasising that the intermediate states are not known. A solid curve means the author is assuming reversibility.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Ein ideales Gas (1 kg Luft, $R = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$) befindet sich im Zustand 1 mit $p_1 = 1\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$. Es durchläuft folgende drei quasistatischen Zustandsänderungen:
>
> - 1 → 2: isotherm auf $p_2 = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$
> - 2 → 3: isobar auf $T_3 = 600\,\mathrm{K}$
> - 3 → 1: direkt zurück zum Ausgangszustand
>
> Skizzieren Sie den Kreisprozess im $(p, v)$-Diagramm und berechnen Sie $v_1$, $v_2$, $v_3$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Gegeben:** Ideal gas, 1 kg air, $R = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$, three state points.

**Step 1. Find the specific volume at each state using $pv = RT$.**

$$v_1 = \frac{R T_1}{p_1} = \frac{287 \cdot 300}{10^5} = 0{,}861\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$$

For state 2 ($T_2 = T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$, $p_2 = 2 \cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{Pa}$):
$$v_2 = \frac{R T_2}{p_2} = \frac{287 \cdot 300}{2 \cdot 10^5} = 0{,}430\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$$

For state 3 ($p_3 = p_2 = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_3 = 600\,\mathrm{K}$):
$$v_3 = \frac{R T_3}{p_3} = \frac{287 \cdot 600}{2 \cdot 10^5} = 0{,}861\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$$

**Step 2. Sketch.**

On a $(p, v)$ diagram:
- State 1: $(v_1, p_1) = (0{,}861\,\mathrm{m^3/kg},\, 1\,\mathrm{bar})$
- State 2: $(v_2, p_2) = (0{,}430\,\mathrm{m^3/kg},\, 2\,\mathrm{bar})$
- State 3: $(v_3, p_3) = (0{,}861\,\mathrm{m^3/kg},\, 2\,\mathrm{bar})$

Process 1 → 2 (isotherm): hyperbola $pv = \mathrm{const}$, moving up and left.

Process 2 → 3 (isobar): horizontal line at $p = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$, moving right.

Process 3 → 1: horizontal at $v = 0{,}861\,\mathrm{m^3/kg}$? No — state 3 has $v_3 = v_1$ but higher $p$ and higher $T$. So to get from 3 to 1, we need an isochoric (constant-$v$) cooling: vertical line at $v = 0{,}861$ moving down.

**Step 3. Classify.**

The three processes are: isotherm (1→2), isobar (2→3), isochor (3→1). The cycle encloses an area on the $(p, v)$ diagram — that area is the net work of the cycle.

</details>

**Klausur pattern to look out for.** A question says: "Compute $q$ and $w$ for this process." Step 1: what IS the process? In practice, examiners always specify the process type, but Klausur-level questions sometimes make you SEPARATE state quantities (computable from endpoints) from process quantities (not).

**Connection forward.** Kapitel 2 formalises this: for any path-independent function $X$ (i.e. state quantity), $\\int_1^2 \\mathrm{d}X = X_2 - X_1$. For path-dependent quantities, the integral $\\int_1^2 \\delta q$ requires you to parametrise the path first.
  `}

**Adiabatic vs. isothermal — the mental model.**

Two knobs: *thermal insulation* (how easily heat crosses) and *speed* (how long the process takes).

- **Thick insulation + any speed** → adiabatic. Q = 0 regardless.
- **Thin insulation + very slow** → isothermal. Heat has time to equilibrate with surroundings.
- **Thin insulation + very fast** → approximately adiabatic anyway. Heat doesn't have time to cross.
- **Thick insulation + very slow** → adiabatic AND reversible → isentropic.

Real compressors live closer to adiabatic because they are fast. Real evaporators/condensers are decidedly NOT adiabatic — they are designed to transfer large amounts of heat.

**Isentropic as the limit.** In thermodynamics problems, the word 'isentropic' almost always appears as the idealised limit against which real machine performance is measured. Writing $s_2 = s_1$ lets you find a reference outlet state, which you then correct with the isentropic efficiency:

$$\\eta_{s,T} = \\frac{h_1 - h_{2,\\text{real}}}{h_1 - h_{2,s}} \\approx 0{,}85\\text{–}0{,}92$$

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** Every process type is a constraint that removes one unknown from your energy balance. Drill the list so you can translate instantly:

- isotherm → $T = \mathrm{const}$, $\Delta U = 0$ (ideal gas only), $\Delta H = 0$ (ideal gas only)
- isobar → $p = \mathrm{const}$, $W = -p\,\Delta V$, $Q = \Delta H$ (closed system)
- isochor → $V = \mathrm{const}$, $W = 0$, $Q = \Delta U$ (closed system)
- isentrop → $s = \mathrm{const}$, $Q = 0$ AND reversible
- adiabat → $Q = 0$, $\Delta U = W$ (closed system)

When the problem names a process, immediately write down what is zero or constant — that one line saves 30 seconds and avoids the single most common mistake of treating heat flow in a nominally adiabatic problem.
