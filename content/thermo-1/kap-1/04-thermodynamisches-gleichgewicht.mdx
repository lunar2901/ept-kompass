<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this concept exists

When we say "a system has temperature $T$ and pressure $p$", we are quietly assuming that $T$ and $p$ have single, well-defined values. But in a lump of gas where the top is hot and the bottom is cold, there is no single $T$ — you would need a function $T(x, y, z, t)$. State variables only make sense for a system in <GermanTerm de="Gleichgewicht" article="das" plural="Gleichgewichte" en="equilibrium" />.

"Equilibrium" in thermodynamics is stricter than it sounds. It is the simultaneous satisfaction of three balances: thermal, mechanical, and chemical. All three have to hold for you to be allowed to write $p$, $T$, $\mu$ as single numbers for the whole system.

This lesson tells you exactly what that means and flags the Klausur traps where students treat a non-equilibrium system as if it were at equilibrium.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The three balances

A system is in *thermodynamic equilibrium* when it is simultaneously in all three of:

1. **Thermisches Gleichgewicht** — temperature is uniform everywhere within the system, and equal to the temperature of any surroundings it is in thermal contact with. Net heat flow across every interior and boundary surface is zero.
2. **Mechanisches Gleichgewicht** — pressure is uniform everywhere (ignoring tiny hydrostatic gradients in slow flows). Net force on every fluid element is zero; nothing is accelerating.
3. **Chemisches Gleichgewicht** — chemical potential of every component is uniform across all phases. Net mass transfer across phase boundaries is zero; no reaction is proceeding.

<Callout variant="note">

If any one of these three fails, the system is in **non-equilibrium**. Its state cannot be described by a single $(T, p, \mu)$ triple — you would need spatial functions $T(x,y,z)$, $p(x,y,z)$, $\mu_i(x,y,z)$ and possibly time dependence too.

</Callout>

### The zeroth law: why temperature is a thing

The <GermanTerm de="nullter Hauptsatz" article="der" en="zeroth law of thermodynamics" /> states:

> If system $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $B$, and $B$ is in thermal equilibrium with system $C$, then $A$ is in thermal equilibrium with $C$.

It sounds trivial. It is not. This transitivity is what lets us define *temperature* as a single number attached to each equilibrium system, and compare the temperatures of different systems using a third system (a thermometer) as a reference.

The zeroth law is the logical foundation for every thermometer. Without it, "temperature" would be an ill-defined pairwise relation, not a property of a single system.

### Local equilibrium — why engineering still works

Real systems almost always have *some* gradient. A heated pipe has temperature gradients along its length, pressure gradients from the pump to the outlet. Strictly, such a system is not at equilibrium.

The practical escape: **local equilibrium**. Divide the system into small parcels. Each parcel is small enough that $T$, $p$ are approximately uniform inside, but large enough to contain enough molecules that statistical averages make sense. We assign $T(x,y,z,t)$, $p(x,y,z,t)$ to each parcel, and use the equilibrium laws on each parcel individually.

This is how all of fluid mechanics, heat transfer, and transport phenomena are built. The justification is that microscopic relaxation times (picoseconds for molecular collisions) are much shorter than macroscopic process times (seconds for flow). Equilibrium holds locally before bulk flow has moved meaningful amounts of matter.

<Callout variant="warning">

Local equilibrium fails in three regimes you will meet later:

- shock waves (gradients steep enough to span a few mean free paths)
- rarefied gases (low density, long mean free paths)
- very fast processes (plasmas, explosive expansion)

For everything in Thermodynamik I, local equilibrium is assumed without comment.

</Callout>

### Equilibrium vs. steady state — do not confuse

- **Gleichgewicht**: no macroscopic changes with time AND no fluxes across any boundary.
- **Stationärer Zustand** (steady state): no macroscopic changes with time at a fixed point, BUT there may be fluxes through the system.

A heated pipe at steady state has constant $T(x)$ at every point, but heat is flowing through the wall. The pipe is NOT at equilibrium (there are gradients, and heat is crossing the boundary); it is in steady state.

Steady-state systems are analysed with the equilibrium framework *locally* at each point, but the overall system is not at equilibrium.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Zwei starre Behälter sind mit einem Rohr und einem geschlossenen Ventil verbunden. Behälter A enthält 2 kg Luft bei $p_A = 4\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_A = 300\,\mathrm{K}$; Behälter B enthält 3 kg Luft bei $p_B = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_B = 300\,\mathrm{K}$. Beide Behälter sind ideal isoliert.
>
> *a)* Sind die beiden Behälter vor dem Öffnen im thermischen, im mechanischen und im chemischen Gleichgewicht miteinander?
> *b)* Was passiert nach dem Öffnen des Ventils (qualitativ)? Welche Art von Ungleichgewicht löst den Prozess aus?
> *c)* Beschreiben Sie den Endzustand. Welches Gleichgewicht ist dann erreicht?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Pre-opening classification.**

- *Thermisch?* $T_A = T_B = 300\,\mathrm{K}$. Same temperature on both sides, so yes, **thermal equilibrium**.
- *Mechanisch?* $p_A = 4\,\mathrm{bar} \neq p_B = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$. Different pressures, so **NOT in mechanical equilibrium** with each other — but note: the valve is closed, so the two systems are isolated from each other. Each separately is in its own internal mechanical equilibrium.
- *Chemisch?* Both contain air (same composition), same $T$. For pure same-composition fluids at the same $T$ and different $p$, the chemical potentials differ (since $\\mu$ depends on $p$). So **NOT in chemical equilibrium** with each other either — but again, only becomes relevant when the valve opens.

**b) What happens after opening.**

The driver is the pressure difference (mechanical ungleichgewicht). Gas flows from A to B until pressures equalise. On a shorter timescale, this flow carries mass, which also transfers composition-uniform-across-both-volumes — but because the compositions were already identical, the chemical-potential gradient disappears with the pressure gradient.

If A and B had different temperatures initially, there would also be a thermal gradient, and heat would flow (plus the mass carrying internal energy). In our case temperatures are equal, but the expansion work may cool the gas leaving A and warm the gas entering B slightly.

**c) Final state.**

Pressure uniform across both tanks: $p_f$ determined by mass balance.

Total mass: $m = m_A + m_B = 5\,\mathrm{kg}$. Total volume: $V = V_A + V_B$, where from the ideal-gas law:

$V_A = m_A R T_A / p_A = 2 \\cdot 287 \\cdot 300 / (4 \\cdot 10^5) = 0{,}4305\\,\\mathrm{m^3}$

$V_B = m_B R T_B / p_B = 3 \\cdot 287 \\cdot 300 / (2 \\cdot 10^5) = 1{,}2915\\,\\mathrm{m^3}$

$V = 1{,}722\\,\\mathrm{m^3}$.

Since the tanks are insulated, rigid, and the combined system is isolated, $U$ is conserved. For an ideal gas $U = m c_v T$, so with total mass and the equal initial temperatures, $T_f = 300\\,\\mathrm{K}$ (no temperature change in the mixed ideal-gas system).

Final pressure: $p_f = m R T_f / V = 5 \\cdot 287 \\cdot 300 / 1{,}722 \\approx 2{,}5 \\cdot 10^5\\,\\mathrm{Pa} = 2{,}5\\,\\mathrm{bar}$.

**Final state:** $T_f = 300\\,\\mathrm{K}$, $p_f = 2{,}5\\,\\mathrm{bar}$, uniform across the combined volume. Now in **simultaneous thermal, mechanical, and chemical equilibrium**.

**Important observation.** The process A → final is irreversible — entropy of the combined system rises, even though $T$ didn't change. The entropy rise comes from the gas from A expanding into the additional volume.

</details>

Let $m_1 = 2\\,\\mathrm{kg}$, $m_2 = 1\\,\\mathrm{kg}$: $T_f = (2 \\cdot 300 + 1 \\cdot 500) / 3 = 1100/3 \\approx 367\\,\\mathrm{K}$. The larger mass pulls the average toward its temperature. In the limit $m_1 \\gg m_2$, $T_f \\to T_1$ — the big tank barely notices the small one. This is why reservoirs are treated as thermal baths in thermodynamics: effectively infinite mass, temperature unchanged.

**Two-scale equilibrium is the default engineering framework.**

Almost every engineering thermodynamics problem uses a two-scale picture:

1. The system being studied is treated as internally at (approximately) uniform state — one $T$, one $p$, one set of $\\mu_i$. This lets us write $U(T, V, n_i)$ as a function of a few variables.
2. The system is coupled to its surroundings via heat, work, or mass flows across a clear boundary. These flows drive the system's state to evolve over time (unsteady problems) or balance (steady-state problems).

This is the *Systemgrenze* picture you met in Lesson 1, now formalised. It works because molecular relaxation happens on nanoseconds while engineering processes unfold on seconds-to-hours: the system internally re-equilibrates far faster than bulk changes.

**When does the two-scale picture break?**

- When the system has internal gradients that can't be ignored. A long insulated pipe has axial temperature gradients; treating the whole pipe as one $T$ is wrong. Solution: divide the pipe into small sections, each with its own local $T$ (this is the starting point of heat-transfer analysis in Wärme- und Stoffübertragung).
- When a phase transition is happening. A boiling pot has two coexisting phases at the same $T$ but different specific enthalpies — the system is 'at equilibrium' (phase equilibrium) but needs more than one state variable to describe.

**Exam framing.** Klausur questions that describe a "cold fluid being heated" or "hot fluid being cooled" always implicitly assume the fluid is internally at equilibrium at each moment. You can read off its temperature, pressure, and specific enthalpy from a single pair of coordinates — even as they change over time. Only questions that explicitly describe "temperature varies with position" are non-equilibrium problems.

## Was kommt als Nächstes

The next lesson — **Zustandsgleichung des idealen Gases** — turns the abstract equilibrium state into a concrete equation. $pV = nRT$ (or $pv = RT$ in specific form) is the first *Zustandsgleichung* (equation of state) we meet. It tells you that for an ideal gas at equilibrium, three state variables are not independent — one is determined by the other two. That is what "only two independent variables" means in practice.
