<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this concept exists

Two previous lessons taught you that a pure-substance state at equilibrium is described by two independent intensive variables. This lesson tells you the explicit formula that connects them for the simplest case: a gas so dilute that molecular interactions can be ignored.

The result, the <GermanTerm de="ideale Gasgleichung" article="die" en="ideal-gas equation" />, is the single most-used equation in Thermodynamik I. Even when you eventually face steam (real, non-ideal) and combustion gases (real, non-ideal), you will often start with the ideal-gas estimate and correct afterward. Write it in your own handwriting ten times until it is muscle memory:

$$
pV = nRT \qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad pv = R T
$$

That is the formula. The rest of this lesson is about when it is accurate, how to use it with engineering units, and the specific-vs-molar form that shows up on steam tables.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The equation, in four equivalent forms

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Ideale Gasgleichung (vier Formen)"
  name_en="Ideal-gas equation (four forms)"
>

All four forms describe the same physics. Choose the form whose units match what the problem gives you.

$$p V = n R T \qquad(1)\text{ — extensive, molar}$$
$$p V = m R_s T \qquad(2)\text{ — extensive, mass-based}$$
$$p v = R_s T \qquad(3)\text{ — specific}$$
$$p V_m = R T \qquad(4)\text{ — molar volume}$$

where:

- $n$ = Stoffmenge (moles), $m$ = Masse (kg)
- $R = 8{,}314\,\mathrm{J/(mol\,K)}$ — universal
- $R_s = R / M$ — specific, depends on the substance; $M$ = molar mass in kg/mol
- $v = V / m$ — specific volume, $V_m = V / n$ — molar volume

</FormulaCard>

For air, $M \approx 0{,}02896\,\mathrm{kg/mol}$, so $R_s = 8{,}314 / 0{,}02896 \approx 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$. **Memorise $R_{s,\text{Luft}} = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$** — it appears in nearly every Klausur problem on air.

For water (steam), $M = 0{,}01802\,\mathrm{kg/mol}$, $R_s \approx 461\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$. For CO₂, $M = 0{,}04401\,\mathrm{kg/mol}$, $R_s \approx 189\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$.

### What "ideal" means

An ideal gas is a model with two assumptions:

1. Gas molecules are point particles (zero volume of their own).
2. Gas molecules do not interact (no intermolecular forces, except during elastic collisions with each other or with the container wall).

Both are approximations. Real molecules have finite size and non-zero attractions. The ideal-gas model is accurate when:

- $p$ is low (wide gas, few molecules per unit volume)
- $T$ is high (kinetic energy dominates over attractive potential energy)
- the gas is far from its critical point / condensation curve

For air at atmospheric conditions, the ideal model is accurate to about 0.1 %. For compressed steam at 100 bar, it might be 10 % off — real-gas corrections matter. For combustion gases at high $T$ and moderate $p$, ideal is usually fine.

### Two Klausur-level manipulations

**Compression at constant $T$ (Boyle's law):**
$$p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2 \quad (T = \mathrm{const})$$

**Heating at constant $V$ (Amontons' law):**
$$\frac{p_1}{T_1} = \frac{p_2}{T_2} \quad (V = \mathrm{const})$$

**Heating at constant $p$ (Charles's law):**
$$\frac{V_1}{T_1} = \frac{V_2}{T_2} \quad (p = \mathrm{const})$$

These are all limiting cases of the full $pV = nRT$. In Klausuren, the problem usually names the process type and you pick the right ratio.

<Callout variant="warning">

Always use **Kelvin** in ideal-gas formulae, never Celsius. 27 °C → 300 K, not 300 °C (= 573 K). Mixing the two gives answers a full order of magnitude wrong.

Always use **Pascals** (or consistent alternative units). 1 bar = 10⁵ Pa. Using bar directly with $R = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$ gives answers 10⁵ too large.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** In einem starren Behälter von $V = 0{,}5\,\mathrm{m^3}$ befinden sich bei $T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ Stickstoff ($M = 0{,}028\,\mathrm{kg/mol}$) unter einem Druck von $p_1 = 4\,\mathrm{bar}$.
>
> *a)* Wie groß ist die Masse des Stickstoffs?
> *b)* Der Behälter wird erhitzt, bis $p_2 = 6\,\mathrm{bar}$ erreicht ist. Welche Endtemperatur $T_2$ stellt sich ein?
> *c)* Welche Wärmemenge $Q$ wurde dem Gas zugeführt, wenn $c_v = 743\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$ angenommen wird?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Mass of nitrogen.**

Use the mass-based form $pV = m R_s T$, with $R_s = R/M = 8{,}314 / 0{,}028 \approx 297\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$.

$$m = \frac{p_1 V}{R_s T_1} = \frac{4 \cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{Pa} \cdot 0{,}5\,\mathrm{m^3}}{297\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)} \cdot 300\,\mathrm{K}}$$

$$m = \frac{2 \cdot 10^5}{89{,}100} \approx 2{,}25\,\mathrm{kg}$$

**b) Final temperature after isochoric heating.**

The container is rigid, so $V_1 = V_2$. Mass is conserved. Divide the state equations:

$$\frac{p_1}{T_1} = \frac{m R_s}{V} = \frac{p_2}{T_2}$$

$$T_2 = T_1 \cdot \frac{p_2}{p_1} = 300 \cdot \frac{6}{4} = 450\,\mathrm{K}$$

**c) Heat added.**

For an isochoric process in a closed system, the first law reads $\Delta U = Q + W$ with $W = 0$ (no volume change). So $Q = \Delta U = m c_v \Delta T$.

$$\Delta T = T_2 - T_1 = 150\,\mathrm{K}$$

$$Q = 2{,}25\,\mathrm{kg} \cdot 743\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)} \cdot 150\,\mathrm{K} = 2{,}51 \cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{J} = 251\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**Sanity check.** Signs: adding heat raises temperature. $\Delta T > 0$ → $Q > 0$ ✓.

</details>

**Why Avogadro's principle is non-obvious.**

Hydrogen molecules are much lighter than nitrogen molecules. It is tempting to think a light molecule would 'fit more' or 'move faster' in a way that affects volume. <Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** In almost every ideal-gas Klausur problem, the moment you read the process type (isotherm, isobar, isochor), one quantity in $pV = nRT$ becomes constant and two stay as unknowns — linked by the ratio law. The three ratios worth memorising:

- isotherm ($T$ const): $p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2$
- isobar ($p$ const): $V_1 / T_1 = V_2 / T_2$
- isochor ($V$ const): $p_1 / T_1 = p_2 / T_2$

Identify the process, pick the ratio, plug in — 30 seconds, no algebra. Save the full $pV = nRT$ for absolute-value calculations like *what mass is in this tank at these conditions*.

</Callout>
