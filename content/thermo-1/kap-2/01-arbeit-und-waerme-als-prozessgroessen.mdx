<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this concept exists

Chapter 1 taught you to describe the *state* of a system: $T$, $p$, $v$, $u$. Chapter 2 teaches you to describe what happens *between* states — the energy flows that connect them. The two flows that matter are <GermanTerm de="Arbeit" article="die" en="work" /> and <GermanTerm de="Wärme" article="die" en="heat" />, and they are **process quantities**, not state quantities.

This distinction is philosophically subtle and practically vital. Energy is stored in a state (as $U$, $H$); it is transferred by a process (as $Q$, $W$). You can ask "how much energy is in this gas" (a state question — answer: $U$) but not "how much heat is in this gas" — that question is meaningless, because heat only exists while crossing a boundary.

This lesson nails down the definitions, sign conventions, and notation conventions that will appear in every first-law problem in the rest of the course.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Work — what it is, and what kinds exist

**Work** is a mode of energy transfer that can be described, at the microscopic level, as an ordered motion: molecules of the system pushed or pulled in a coordinated way by an external force. A piston moving against gas pressure, a shaft rotating, current flowing through a resistor inside the system — all are work modes.

Four kinds of work appear repeatedly in Thermodynamik I:

| Type | Notation | Typical context |
| --- | --- | --- |
| <GermanTerm de="Volumenänderungsarbeit" article="die" en="boundary work" /> | $W_V$ or $W_{pV}$ | piston-cylinder, closed system with moving wall |
| <GermanTerm de="Wellenarbeit" article="die" en="shaft work" /> | $W_W$ or $P$ (rate) | turbines, compressors, stirrers |
| elektrische Arbeit | $W_{\text{el}}$ | heaters, electrochemistry |
| <GermanTerm de="technische Arbeit" article="die" en="technical work" /> | $W_t$ | open systems, sum of shaft + flow work + kin/pot. |

Not every problem has every type. For a sealed rigid tank being heated, $W = 0$ (no moving boundary, no shaft). For a turbine, $W_V = 0$ (control volume fixed in space) but shaft work dominates.

### Heat — what it is

**Heat** is a mode of energy transfer driven by a temperature difference and carried at the molecular level by disordered motion: molecules on the hot side bump molecules on the cold side, transferring kinetic energy without any net organised flow. The three physical mechanisms are **conduction** (molecule-to-molecule), **convection** (fluid motion carries warm matter), and **radiation** (electromagnetic waves).

For Thermodynamik I's energy balances we don't care about the mechanism — we just care about the total $Q$ crossing the boundary. Mechanism matters in *Wärme- und Stoffübertragung*.

### Sign convention — from the system's perspective

<Callout variant="note">

**The EPT-Kompass / German engineering convention.**

- $Q > 0$: heat flows *into* the system.
- $Q < 0$: heat flows *out of* the system.
- $W > 0$: work is done *on* the system (system's energy increases).
- $W < 0$: work is done *by* the system on its surroundings.

Both positive means energy is entering the system. This is the convention in every German Thermodynamik textbook (Baehr, Stephan) and on TU Berlin Klausuren. *Be aware* that some older US/UK textbooks use the opposite convention for work: $W > 0$ means work done *by* the system. Always verify the convention before starting a problem.

</Callout>

### The exact-vs-inexact differential notation

Because $U$, $H$, $S$, $V$ are state quantities, their infinitesimal changes are *exact differentials*, written with roman $\mathrm{d}$:

$$\mathrm{d}U,\quad \mathrm{d}H,\quad \mathrm{d}S,\quad \mathrm{d}V$$

Because $Q$ and $W$ are process quantities, their infinitesimal amounts are *inexact differentials*, written with $\delta$ (or the crossed-d in older German texts):

$$\delta Q,\quad \delta W$$

<Callout variant="warning">

**Do not write $\mathrm{d}Q$ or $\mathrm{d}W$.** These expressions are mathematically incorrect, and Klausur examiners deduct marks. The reason: $\mathrm{d}$ signals "I can integrate this and get a state-quantity difference depending only on endpoints", but heat and work do NOT have that property. Writing $\int_1^2 \delta Q$ is fine; writing $Q_2 - Q_1$ is nonsense because $Q$ does not have a value at a state.

</Callout>

### Volumenänderungsarbeit — the explicit formula

The single most important work-rate formula in the chapter. For a gas in a piston-cylinder during a *quasi-static* process, the infinitesimal work the system does *on* the piston is $p \, \mathrm{d}V$; by our sign convention this is work done BY the system, so $\delta W = -p \, \mathrm{d}V$ when $W$ is measured as "work ON the system":

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Volumenänderungsarbeit (geschlossenes System, quasistatisch)"
  name_en="Boundary work (closed system, quasi-static)"
  latex="W_{V,12} = -\int_{V_1}^{V_2} p \, \mathrm{d}V"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "W_{V,12}", name_de: "Volumenänderungsarbeit von 1 nach 2", name_en: "boundary work from 1 to 2", unit: "J" },
    { symbol: "p", name_de: "Druck des Systems", name_en: "system pressure", unit: "Pa" },
    { symbol: "V", name_de: "Volumen des Systems", name_en: "system volume", unit: "m³" },
  ]}
>

- For *compression* ($V_2 < V_1$), the integral goes "backwards", so $W_V > 0$ — positive work done on the gas, consistent with our sign convention.
- For *expansion* ($V_2 > V_1$), $W_V < 0$ — gas does work on the surroundings.

The minus sign is not a quirk — it comes directly from the sign convention. In US/UK textbooks where $W > 0$ means "by the system", the sign is $W = +\int p \, \mathrm{d}V$. Same physics, opposite sign.

</FormulaCard>

### Specialised formulas for iso-processes

By evaluating $-\int p \, \mathrm{d}V$ for each process type (with $p(V)$ from the ideal-gas law), you get clean closed-form expressions:

| Process | Constraint | $W_V$ (closed system, ideal gas) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| isochor | $V = \mathrm{const}$ | $W_V = 0$ |
| isobar | $p = \mathrm{const}$ | $W_V = -p(V_2 - V_1) = -p \, \Delta V$ |
| isotherm | $T = \mathrm{const}$, $pv = RT$ | $W_V = -m R_s T \ln(V_2/V_1) = m R_s T \ln(p_2/p_1)$ |
| isentrop | $pV^\kappa = \mathrm{const}$ | $W_V = \dfrac{p_2 V_2 - p_1 V_1}{\kappa - 1}$ |

**Memorise the isothermal and isobaric forms.** They appear in every closed-system Klausur.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** 2 kg Luft ($R_s = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$) werden in einem Kolben-Zylinder isotherm bei $T = 400\,\mathrm{K}$ von $V_1 = 0{,}5\,\mathrm{m^3}$ auf $V_2 = 0{,}2\,\mathrm{m^3}$ verdichtet. Berechnen Sie:
>
> *a)* Die Anfangs- und Enddrücke.
> *b)* Die Volumenänderungsarbeit $W_V$.
> *c)* Die Wärme $Q$, die bei dieser isothermen Verdichtung abgegeben oder aufgenommen wird.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Gegeben:** 2 kg air, isothermal, $T = 400\,\mathrm{K}$, $V_1 = 0{,}5\,\mathrm{m^3}$, $V_2 = 0{,}2\,\mathrm{m^3}$, $R_s = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$.

**Step 1. Pressures from $pV = m R_s T$.**

$$p_1 = \frac{m R_s T}{V_1} = \frac{2 \cdot 287 \cdot 400}{0{,}5} = 4{,}59 \cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{Pa} = 4{,}59\,\mathrm{bar}$$

$$p_2 = \frac{m R_s T}{V_2} = \frac{2 \cdot 287 \cdot 400}{0{,}2} = 1{,}148 \cdot 10^6\,\mathrm{Pa} = 11{,}48\,\mathrm{bar}$$

**Step 2. Volumenänderungsarbeit (isothermal).**

$$W_V = -\int_{V_1}^{V_2} p \, \mathrm{d}V = -m R_s T \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{V} = -m R_s T \ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)$$

$$W_V = -2 \cdot 287 \cdot 400 \cdot \ln(0{,}2 / 0{,}5) = -229\,600 \cdot \ln(0{,}4) \approx -229\,600 \cdot (-0{,}916)$$

$$W_V \approx +2{,}10 \cdot 10^5\,\mathrm{J} = 210\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

Positive: we are compressing, which means pushing on the gas, which is work done ON the gas. Consistent with the sign convention.

**Step 3. Heat from the first law.**

For an ideal gas at constant $T$, $\Delta U = 0$ (internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on $T$). First law closed system:

$$\Delta U = Q + W_V \quad\Rightarrow\quad 0 = Q + W_V \quad\Rightarrow\quad Q = -W_V = -210\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

Negative: heat leaves the system. Physically expected — isothermal compression requires continuously removing heat to keep $T$ constant, otherwise the gas would warm from the compression work.

**Summary:** 210 kJ of work goes in, 210 kJ of heat goes out, internal energy unchanged. The isothermal compression is a 100 % conversion of input work to output heat.

</details>

**The isothermal work formula — pressure vs volume form.**

Two equivalent expressions:

$$W_V = -m R_s T \\ln\\!\\left(\\frac{V_2}{V_1}\\right) = m R_s T \\ln\\!\\left(\\frac{p_2}{p_1}\\right)$$

Both correct. Use the one whose arguments are easier to read off the problem. If the problem gives you $V_1$, $V_2$, use the volume form. If it gives you $p_1$, $p_2$ (common for Klausuren where mass and T are specified), use the pressure form — no ideal-gas-law step needed to convert volumes to pressures.

**Why the two forms are equivalent.** For an ideal gas at constant $T$: $p_1 V_1 = p_2 V_2 = mR_sT$, so $V_2/V_1 = p_1/p_2$, and $\\ln(V_2/V_1) = -\\ln(p_2/p_1)$. The minus sign moves outside the log, cancels the one in front of the formula, and you get the pressure form.

**Energy bookkeeping for the isothermal compression.**

- $W_V = +138{,}6\\,\\mathrm{kJ}$ (work into gas via piston)
- $Q = -138{,}6\\,\\mathrm{kJ}$ (heat out through cylinder wall)
- $\\Delta U = 0$ (temperature constant, so internal energy unchanged for ideal gas)

100 % of the work input is exported as heat. The gas is essentially a "pass-through" converting mechanical work to thermal energy — not useful as a heat engine, but characteristic of very slow, cooled compression in an idealised heat exchanger.

**Why real compressors are not isothermal.**

Industrial compressors operate on millisecond-to-second timescales. Heat transfer through the cylinder walls is too slow to extract the compression work as heat. So real compressors are closer to *adiabatic* (more precisely: isentropic if ideal, or nearly so with 85-90 % isentropic efficiency for real machines). Isothermal compression is the theoretical lower-energy bound; real machines need more work input than the isothermal minimum because they also heat the gas (which then has to be cooled afterward — usually via an intercooler between stages).

**Klausur-Tipp.** At the start of every first-law problem, write three lines:

1. *Systemgrenze:* what is inside the system you're analysing?
2. *Vorzeichenkonvention:* state the convention (EPT-Kompass: $Q$, $W > 0$ means into the system).
3. *Prozesstyp:* isotherm, isobar, isochor, adiabat, polytrop, or other.

Those three lines take 30 seconds and prevent 90 % of first-law errors. The examiner sees them and awards partial credit even if your algebra goes wrong — they prove you understood the setup.
