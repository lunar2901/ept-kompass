## Why this concept exists

Closed systems cover pistons and sealed tanks. Power plants, jet engines, chemical plants, and the Berlin district-heating network all run on open systems — matter flows in, matter flows out, and usually at a *steady state* (time-independent at each point).

The closed-system first law $\Delta U = Q + W$ doesn't fit directly: the mass inside the control volume doesn't change, so "ΔU" isn't the right quantity. What changes is the specific enthalpy of the flowing matter as it passes through. This lesson writes down the open-system first law in the form every Klausur uses:

$$
\dot{Q} + P = \dot{m}\left[(h_2 - h_1) + \tfrac{1}{2}(c_2^2 - c_1^2) + g(z_2 - z_1)\right]
$$

Most Klausur problems drop the kinetic and potential terms, leaving the clean $\dot{Q} + P = \dot{m} \Delta h$. Master the full form for when you need it.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### Mass balance (conservation of mass)

In steady state, the mass inside the control volume doesn't change with time. So total mass inflow equals total mass outflow:

$$\sum_{\text{in}} \dot{m}_i = \sum_{\text{out}} \dot{m}_j$$

For one-in-one-out devices (turbines, compressors, pumps, valves): $\dot{m}_1 = \dot{m}_2 = \dot{m}$.

### Energy balance (first law for steady-flow)

Sign convention: $\dot{Q} > 0$ → heat into CV; $P > 0$ → technical work into CV (compressor, pump); $P < 0$ → out (turbine, expander).

For one-in-one-out:

$$\dot{Q} + P = \dot{m}\left[(h_2 - h_1) + \tfrac{1}{2}(c_2^2 - c_1^2) + g(z_2 - z_1)\right]$$

### When can we drop the KE and PE terms?

Almost always, in Thermodynamik I. Compare the magnitudes:

- **Enthalpy difference** for a turbine stage: $\Delta h \sim 500\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$.
- **Kinetic-energy change**: a fluid accelerating from 50 m/s to 100 m/s has $\Delta(c^2/2) = (100^2 - 50^2)/2 = 3750\,\mathrm{J/kg} = 3{,}75\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$ — less than 1 % of typical $\Delta h$.
- **Potential-energy change**: even a 100 m elevation change is $g \Delta z = 9{,}81 \cdot 100 \approx 1\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$ — negligible for most plant equipment.

Exceptions where you must keep them:
- **Nozzles and diffusers** — the whole point is changing velocity, so KE is THE variable.
- **Hydraulic systems** (water at moderate temperatures) — $\Delta h$ can be small, and PE matters over tall vertical runs.

### Typical simplifications for common devices

| Device | Dominant term | Simplification |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Turbine** | $-P$ output | $-P = \dot{m}(h_1 - h_2)$, adiabatic |
| **Verdichter** (compressor) | $+P$ input | $+P = \dot{m}(h_2 - h_1)$, adiabatic |
| **Pumpe** | $+P$ small | often $P \approx \dot{m} v \Delta p$ for liquids |
| **Drossel** (throttle) | none | $h_1 = h_2$ (isenthalpic) |
| **Wärmetauscher** | $\dot{Q}$ | $\dot{Q} = \dot{m} (h_2 - h_1)$, no work |
| **Düse** (nozzle) | KE | $\tfrac{1}{2}(c_2^2 - c_1^2) = h_1 - h_2$ |

Each of these is a specialisation of the one general balance with different terms set to zero. Memorise the general form; derive the specialised forms when you need them.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** In einer adiabaten Dampfturbine tritt Wasserdampf mit $\dot{m} = 10\,\mathrm{kg/s}$, $h_1 = 3400\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$, $c_1 = 50\,\mathrm{m/s}$ ein und verlässt die Turbine mit $h_2 = 2600\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$, $c_2 = 180\,\mathrm{m/s}$. Höhenunterschiede sind vernachlässigbar.
>
> *a)* Welche Leistung $P$ gibt die Turbine ab (in MW)?
> *b)* Welchen Anteil liefern die kinetischen Terme zur Leistungsabgabe?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Setup.** Turbine → adiabatic → $\dot{Q} = 0$. Elevation change → negligible.

Energy balance:
$$0 + P = \dot{m}\left[(h_2 - h_1) + \tfrac{1}{2}(c_2^2 - c_1^2)\right]$$

**a) Power.**

$\Delta h = h_2 - h_1 = 2600 - 3400 = -800\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$ (enthalpy drops across turbine).

$\Delta(c^2/2) = (180^2 - 50^2)/2 = (32\,400 - 2500)/2 = 14\,950\,\mathrm{J/kg} = 14{,}95\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$.

Total per kg: $-800 + 14{,}95 = -785{,}05\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$.

$$P = \dot{m} \cdot (-785{,}05\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}) = 10 \cdot (-785{,}05) = -7850{,}5\,\mathrm{kW} \approx -7{,}85\,\mathrm{MW}$$

Negative → work OUT of the control volume → turbine delivers work. Magnitude: **7.85 MW**.

**b) Kinetic contribution.**

KE term contributes $10 \cdot 14{,}95 = 149{,}5\,\mathrm{kW}$ to the magnitude (with the opposite sign — it reduces the delivered power, because the exit flow has more KE than the inlet). Relative to the enthalpy contribution (8000 kW), the KE is $\approx 1{,}9\,\%$. Small but not negligible for a precise design.

</details>

**Applications.**

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For open-system Klausur problems:

1. Draw the CV (control volume) box around the device.
2. Identify inlets and outlets; label $\dot{m}$ at each.
3. Write $\dot{Q} + P = \dot{m}[\Delta h + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta c^2 + g\Delta z]$ — always, every time.
4. Cross out terms that are zero (adiabatic → $\dot{Q} = 0$; no shaft → $P = 0$; small velocities → KE = 0).
5. Solve.

Writing the full form and crossing out is the examiner's best signal that you know the theory. Fill in only the terms the problem requires.

</Callout>
