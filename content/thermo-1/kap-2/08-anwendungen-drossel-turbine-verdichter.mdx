## Why this concept exists

Seven lessons of groundwork land here: the three archetypal open-system devices — <GermanTerm de="Drossel" article="die" en="throttle" />, <GermanTerm de="Turbine" article="die" en="turbine" />, <GermanTerm de="Verdichter" article="der" en="compressor" /> — and the recipe for attacking any Klausur problem built around them.

Every Thermodynamik I Klausur has at least one of these three. They appear in the Clausius-Rankine cycle (steam plants), the Brayton cycle (gas turbines, jet engines), vapour-compression refrigeration, heat pumps, and any process with flowing fluids. This lesson is what you review the morning of the exam.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The three archetypes at a glance

| Device | Purpose | Key simplifications | Key equation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Drossel** | pressure drop, no work | adiabatic, no work, no KE/PE | $h_2 = h_1$ |
| **Turbine** | extract shaft work from flow | adiabatic, no KE/PE | $P = \dot{m}(h_2 - h_1) < 0$ |
| **Verdichter** | add shaft work to raise pressure | adiabatic, no KE/PE | $P = \dot{m}(h_2 - h_1) > 0$ |

All three use the open-system first law in its simplest form: $P = \dot{m} \Delta h$, with $P = 0$ for the throttle.

### Throttle details

A throttle is a flow restriction (a valve, a porous plug, a capillary tube) that drops pressure. Adiabatic (fast, insulated), no shaft, small velocity change. The first law reduces to $h_2 = h_1$ — isenthalpic.

Physical effect for a **real gas** (Joule-Thomson): temperature changes even though $h$ is constant, because $h$ depends on both $T$ and $p$ for a real gas. For most gases below their inversion temperature, throttling *cools* the gas — the basis of the Linde liquefaction process. For an ideal gas, $h = h(T)$ alone, so $T$ is also conserved.

### Turbine details — isentropic efficiency

A real turbine is adiabatic (approximately) but NOT reversible — irreversibilities (friction, turbulence) reduce the work extracted per unit enthalpy drop. Engineers quantify this with the **isentropic efficiency**:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Isentroper Wirkungsgrad (Turbine)"
  name_en="Isentropic efficiency (turbine)"
  latex="\eta_{s,T} = \frac{h_1 - h_{2,\text{real}}}{h_1 - h_{2,s}}"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "h_1", name_de: "Eintrittsenthalpie", name_en: "inlet enthalpy", unit: "J/kg" },
    { symbol: "h_{2,s}", name_de: "Austrittsenthalpie bei isentropem Idealprozess", name_en: "outlet enthalpy if isentropic", unit: "J/kg" },
    { symbol: "h_{2,\\text{real}}", name_de: "tatsächliche Austrittsenthalpie", name_en: "actual outlet enthalpy", unit: "J/kg" },
  ]}
>

$\eta_{s,T} < 1$ always. Typical values for large steam turbines: 0.85–0.92. For small gas turbines: 0.75–0.85.

Real extracted work: $|w_{t,\text{real}}| = \eta_{s,T} \cdot |w_{t,\text{ideal}}|$ — less than the isentropic ideal.

</FormulaCard>

### Compressor details — isentropic efficiency (inverted)

For a compressor, the real work is MORE than the isentropic ideal (because irreversibilities heat the gas, requiring more compression work than an ideal isentropic compression at the same pressures). So the efficiency is defined as the ratio in the other direction:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Isentroper Wirkungsgrad (Verdichter)"
  name_en="Isentropic efficiency (compressor)"
  latex="\eta_{s,V} = \frac{h_{2,s} - h_1}{h_{2,\text{real}} - h_1}"
>

Again $\eta_{s,V} < 1$. Typical values: 0.75–0.88 for large compressors.

Real required work: $|w_{t,\text{real}}| = |w_{t,\text{ideal}}| / \eta_{s,V}$ — more than the isentropic ideal.

</FormulaCard>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Luft ($c_p = 1005\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$, $\kappa = 1{,}4$, $R_s = 287$) strömt in einem gas-Turbinen-Aggregat durch:
>
> 1. **Verdichter** ($\eta_{s,V} = 0{,}85$): $p_1 = 1\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ → $p_2 = 10\,\mathrm{bar}$.
> 2. **Brennkammer** (isobar, nicht Teil dieser Aufgabe): $T_3 = 1400\,\mathrm{K}$ nach Brennkammer.
> 3. **Turbine** ($\eta_{s,T} = 0{,}90$): $p_3 = 10\,\mathrm{bar}$ → $p_4 = 1\,\mathrm{bar}$.
>
> Mit einem Massenstrom $\dot{m} = 50\,\mathrm{kg/s}$, berechnen Sie: *a)* Verdichterleistung $P_V$. *b)* Turbinenleistung $P_T$. *c)* Netto-Leistung.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Verdichter.**

Isentropic outlet temp: $T_{2,s} = 300 \cdot 10^{0{,}2857} \approx 579{,}2\,\mathrm{K}$.

Ideal isentropic work: $w_{t,V,s} = c_p (T_{2,s} - T_1) = 1005 \cdot 279{,}2 = 280{,}600\,\mathrm{J/kg}$.

Real work: $w_{t,V,\text{real}} = w_{s} / \eta_{s,V} = 280\,600 / 0{,}85 = 330\,100\,\mathrm{J/kg} \approx 330\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$.

Compressor power: $P_V = \dot{m} \cdot w_{t,V,\text{real}} = 50 \cdot 330 = 16\,500\,\mathrm{kW} = 16{,}5\,\mathrm{MW}$.

**Turbine.**

Isentropic outlet: $T_{4,s} = T_3 \cdot (p_4/p_3)^{(\kappa-1)/\kappa} = 1400 \cdot (1/10)^{0{,}2857} = 1400 \cdot 0{,}518 \approx 725\,\mathrm{K}$.

Ideal isentropic work extraction: $|w_{t,T,s}| = c_p (T_3 - T_{4,s}) = 1005 \cdot (1400 - 725) = 1005 \cdot 675 = 678\,400\,\mathrm{J/kg} \approx 678\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$.

Real extracted work: $|w_{t,T,\text{real}}| = \eta_{s,T} \cdot |w_{t,T,s}| = 0{,}90 \cdot 678 = 610\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$.

Turbine power: $|P_T| = \dot{m} \cdot 610 = 50 \cdot 610 = 30\,500\,\mathrm{kW} = 30{,}5\,\mathrm{MW}$.

**Net power.**

$P_{\text{net}} = |P_T| - P_V = 30{,}5 - 16{,}5 = 14{,}0\,\mathrm{MW}$.

This is the shaft output available for driving a generator or a propeller. The 16.5 MW compressor power comes from the turbine; the remaining 14 MW is the "useful" output.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp — the universal open-system attack pattern.**

Every open-system problem boils down to this sequence:

1. Classify each device (throttle / turbine / compressor / heat-exchanger).
2. Apply the open-system first law, crossed-out of useless terms.
3. For adiabatic devices: use $h_2 = h_1$ (throttle) OR $P = \dot{m}(h_2 - h_1)$ (turbine/compressor).
4. For non-ideal machines: apply $\eta_s$ correctly (multiply for turbine, divide for compressor).
5. Multiply by $\dot{m}$ to get power; sum over devices to get net power.

Memorise this sequence cold. It handles the first-law section of every Thermodynamik I Klausur.

</Callout>
