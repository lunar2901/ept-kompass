<Callout variant="intuition">

## Why this concept exists

Heat and work enter a system. Something inside stores what they leave behind. That something is <GermanTerm de="innere Energie" article="die" en="internal energy" />, $U$ — the energy content of the matter inside the system boundary.

For a Klausur, two things about $U$ are worth knowing cold:

1. $U$ is a **state quantity**: $\Delta U = U_2 - U_1$ depends only on endpoints.
2. For an **ideal gas**, $U = U(T)$ only — it does not depend on $p$ or $v$.

Point (2) is the reason ideal-gas problems are so much simpler than real-gas problems. If you know $T_1$ and $T_2$, you know $\Delta U$, regardless of what path the gas took.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The molecular picture

At the molecular level, internal energy is the sum of every kinetic and potential energy inside the system boundary:

- Translational kinetic energy of every molecule (random motion in 3D)
- Rotational kinetic energy (for non-monatomic molecules)
- Vibrational kinetic + potential energy (for molecules with flexible bonds)
- Intermolecular potential energy (from attractions and repulsions)
- Electronic energy (usually frozen at normal temperatures)

For an ideal gas, intermolecular potential energy is zero (by assumption). For a monatomic ideal gas (He, Ar), only translation contributes — $\frac{3}{2} k_B T$ per molecule. Adding up over all molecules: $U = \frac{3}{2} n R T$ for the system.

For diatomic and polyatomic gases, rotation and vibration add further contributions, but in every case for an ideal gas, $U$ depends on $T$ alone.

### The thermodynamic definition

On a macroscopic level, $U$ is defined by the first law. Starting from a reference state where $U = 0$, perform any sequence of heat and work transfers; the change in $U$ is $\Delta U = Q + W$. Since $U$ is a state quantity, any two paths between the same endpoints give the same $\Delta U$.

### How $U$ depends on $T$ — specific heat at constant volume

For any substance, the rate at which $U$ changes with $T$ at constant volume is called the *specific heat capacity at constant volume*:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Spezifische Wärmekapazität bei konstantem Volumen"
  name_en="Specific heat capacity at constant volume"
  latex="c_v = \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial T}\right)_v"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "c_v", name_de: "spezifische Wärmekapazität bei V = const", name_en: "specific heat capacity at constant V", unit: "J/(kg·K)" },
    { symbol: "u", name_de: "spezifische innere Energie", name_en: "specific internal energy", unit: "J/kg" },
    { symbol: "T", name_de: "Temperatur", name_en: "temperature", unit: "K" },
  ]}
>

For an ideal gas (where $u = u(T)$ only), the partial derivative becomes a total derivative: $c_v = \mathrm{d}u/\mathrm{d}T$. Integrating:

$$\Delta u = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} c_v(T) \, \mathrm{d}T \approx c_v \cdot \Delta T$$

(the approximation is exact for a temperature-independent $c_v$, which is a very good approximation for ideal gases over modest $T$ ranges).

**Typical values for air** at room conditions: $c_v \approx 718\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$, $c_p \approx 1005\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$.

</FormulaCard>

### Mayer's relation — the $c_p - c_v$ difference

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Mayer-Relation (ideales Gas)"
  name_en="Mayer's relation (ideal gas)"
  latex="c_p - c_v = R_s"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "c_p", name_de: "spezifische Wärmekapazität bei p = const", name_en: "specific heat capacity at constant p", unit: "J/(kg·K)" },
    { symbol: "c_v", name_de: "spezifische Wärmekapazität bei V = const", name_en: "specific heat capacity at constant V", unit: "J/(kg·K)" },
    { symbol: "R_s", name_de: "spezifische Gaskonstante", name_en: "specific gas constant", unit: "J/(kg·K)" },
  ]}
>

Quick verification for air: $1005 - 718 = 287 = R_{s,\text{Luft}}$. ✓

The physical meaning: at constant $p$, heating raises $T$ AND expands the gas (boundary work is done against the constant external pressure). At constant $V$, only $T$ rises — no boundary work. $c_p > c_v$ because at constant $p$ some of the input heat goes to work, not just to raising $T$.

</FormulaCard>

### The adiabatic index $\kappa$

The ratio $\kappa = c_p / c_v$ appears in every formula involving isentropic (adiabatic + reversible) processes:

- Monatomic ideal gas (He, Ar): $\kappa = 5/3 \approx 1.667$
- Diatomic ideal gas (N₂, O₂, air): $\kappa \approx 1.4$
- Triatomic / polyatomic (CO₂, H₂O steam as ideal gas): $\kappa \approx 1.3$

For every isentropic process on an ideal gas: $pV^\kappa = \mathrm{const}$, $T V^{\kappa - 1} = \mathrm{const}$, $T^\kappa \, p^{1-\kappa} = \mathrm{const}$.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** 3 kg Luft (ideal, $c_v = 718\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$) werden von $T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ isochor erhitzt auf $T_2 = 500\,\mathrm{K}$. Berechnen Sie:
>
> *a)* Die Änderung der inneren Energie $\Delta U$.
> *b)* Die zugeführte Wärme $Q$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) $\Delta U$** for an ideal gas depends only on the temperature change:

$$\Delta U = m \cdot c_v \cdot \Delta T = 3 \cdot 718 \cdot (500 - 300) = 3 \cdot 718 \cdot 200$$

$$\Delta U = 430\,800\,\mathrm{J} \approx 431\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**b) First law for closed, isochoric system.** $\Delta V = 0$ means $W_V = 0$. Only heat enters:

$$\Delta U = Q + W_V = Q + 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad Q = \Delta U \approx 431\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**Observation.** For any isochoric heating of an ideal gas, $Q = \Delta U = m c_v \Delta T$. For isobaric heating, by contrast, $Q = \Delta H = m c_p \Delta T$. The difference, $(c_p - c_v) \Delta T \cdot m = R_s \, m \, \Delta T$, is the boundary work the isobaric process does on the surroundings.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** The two specific-heat formulas you'll use most:

$$\Delta U = m \cdot c_v \cdot \Delta T \qquad \Delta H = m \cdot c_p \cdot \Delta T$$

Both are valid for **any** ideal-gas process (not just the "iso" whose name they share). The subscript says which derivative defined them, NOT which process they apply to. This single clarification prevents a third of first-law Klausur mistakes.

## Was kommt als Nächstes

Next lesson — **Enthalpie $H$** — generalises internal energy for systems with flowing matter. $H = U + pV$ absorbs the "flow work" $pV$ needed to push matter across a boundary, which is why open-system energy balances are written in $h$, not $u$. Once $H$ is defined, the first law for open systems becomes a short step away.
