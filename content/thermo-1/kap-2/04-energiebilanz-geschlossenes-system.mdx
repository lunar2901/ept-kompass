## Why this concept exists

Three lessons of parts now assemble into one equation. The first law for a closed system:

$$
\Delta U = Q + W
$$

says: the change in stored internal energy equals the heat in plus the work in. Every closed-system problem in Thermodynamik I is solved by setting up this equation, plugging in the process-specific specialisation for $Q$, $W$, and $\Delta U$, and solving for whatever is unknown.

This lesson puts it all together in one place. You leave with a one-page cheat sheet that handles isochoric, isobaric, isothermal, and adiabatic processes on ideal gases — the four workhorses.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The first law in its closed-system form

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Erster Hauptsatz für geschlossene Systeme"
  name_en="First law for closed systems"
  latex="\mathrm{d}U = \delta Q + \delta W \quad \Rightarrow \quad \Delta U = U_2 - U_1 = Q_{12} + W_{12}"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "U", name_de: "innere Energie (Zustandsgröße)", name_en: "internal energy (state variable)", unit: "J" },
    { symbol: "Q_{12}", name_de: "zugeführte Wärme 1→2", name_en: "heat added 1→2", unit: "J" },
    { symbol: "W_{12}", name_de: "verrichtete Arbeit 1→2", name_en: "work done 1→2", unit: "J" },
  ]}
>

Sign convention (EPT-Kompass): $Q > 0$ → heat into system; $W > 0$ → work into system. Both positive = energy entering.

For any work type (boundary, shaft, electrical), $W$ in this equation means the total of all work crossing the boundary during the process. In Kapitel 2 we focus on closed systems where the only work mode is <GermanTerm de="Volumenänderungsarbeit" article="die" en="boundary work" /> $W_V = -\int p\,\mathrm{d}V$ — no shaft through the cylinder wall, no electrical feed-through.

</FormulaCard>

### The four-row ideal-gas cheat sheet

For an ideal gas in a closed system, pick a row by matching the process type:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Bilanztabelle für iso-Prozesse (ideales Gas, geschlossen)"
  name_en="Balance table for iso-processes (ideal gas, closed)"
>

| Process | Constraint | $W_V$ | $\Delta U$ | $Q$ |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **isochor** | $V = \mathrm{const}$ | $0$ | $m c_v \Delta T$ | $m c_v \Delta T$ |
| **isobar** | $p = \mathrm{const}$ | $-p\,\Delta V$ | $m c_v \Delta T$ | $m c_p \Delta T$ |
| **isotherm** | $T = \mathrm{const}$ | $m R_s T \ln(p_2/p_1)$ | $0$ | $-W_V$ |
| **isentrop** / adiabat-reversibel | $s = \mathrm{const}$ | $m c_v \Delta T$ | $m c_v \Delta T$ | $0$ |

</FormulaCard>

Three quick observations from the table:

1. $\Delta U = m c_v \Delta T$ on every row (ideal gas). It is a state-quantity shortcut: $U$ depends only on $T$.
2. $Q = 0$ on the adiabat row, forcing $W_V = \Delta U$. That is what makes adiabatic compression calculations quick.
3. On the isotherm row, $\Delta U = 0$, so $Q = -W_V$. Compression work equals heat rejected, and the gas is a pass-through.

### The seven-step first-law protocol

For any closed-system problem:

1. Draw the system + *Systemgrenze*.
2. State the sign convention.
3. Identify the process type (isotherm, isobar, isochor, adiabat, polytrop, or given by $p(V)$ explicitly).
4. Write $\Delta U = Q + W$.
5. Plug in $\Delta U = m c_v \Delta T$ (ideal gas).
6. Plug in $W_V$ from the iso-process formula.
7. Solve for the unknown.

Follow this order and the algebra almost writes itself.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Eine Menge von $m = 1{,}5\,\mathrm{kg}$ Luft ($c_v = 718\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$, $c_p = 1005\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$, $R_s = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$) wird in einem Kolben-Zylinder zunächst isobar bei $p_1 = 2\,\mathrm{bar}$ von $T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ auf $T_2 = 500\,\mathrm{K}$ erwärmt. Berechnen Sie für diesen Prozess: $V_1$, $V_2$, $W_V$, $\Delta U$, $Q$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**Step 1. Initial and final volumes.**

$$V_1 = \frac{m R_s T_1}{p_1} = \frac{1{,}5 \cdot 287 \cdot 300}{2 \cdot 10^5} = 0{,}6458\,\mathrm{m^3}$$

$$V_2 = \frac{m R_s T_2}{p_1} = \frac{1{,}5 \cdot 287 \cdot 500}{2 \cdot 10^5} = 1{,}0763\,\mathrm{m^3}$$

**Step 2. Boundary work (isobaric).**

$$W_V = -p_1 (V_2 - V_1) = -2 \cdot 10^5 \cdot (1{,}0763 - 0{,}6458) = -2 \cdot 10^5 \cdot 0{,}4305$$

$$W_V = -86\,100\,\mathrm{J} = -86{,}1\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

Negative: the gas expanded, doing work on the surroundings.

**Step 3. Internal energy change.**

$$\Delta U = m c_v \Delta T = 1{,}5 \cdot 718 \cdot 200 = 215\,400\,\mathrm{J} \approx 215{,}4\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**Step 4. Heat from the first law.**

$$Q = \Delta U - W_V = 215\,400 - (-86\,100) = 301\,500\,\mathrm{J} \approx 301{,}5\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**Verification.** For isobaric processes, $Q = m c_p \Delta T = 1{,}5 \cdot 1005 \cdot 200 = 301\,500\,\mathrm{J}$. ✓

301.5 kJ of heat enters the gas; 215.4 kJ is stored as internal energy (temperature rise); 86.1 kJ is exported as boundary work as the piston pushes out.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For iso-process closed-system problems with an ideal gas, memorise three one-liners:

- isobar: $W_V = -m R_s \Delta T$
- isotherm: $W_V = m R_s T \ln(p_2/p_1)$
- isochor: $W_V = 0$

Plus $\Delta U = m c_v \Delta T$ on every row, $\Delta H = m c_p \Delta T$ on every row. The first law $\Delta U = Q + W$ closes the bookkeeping. Total memorisation: five formulas. That handles 80 % of Kapitel 2 Klausur questions.
