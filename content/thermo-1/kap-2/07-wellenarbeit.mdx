## Why this concept exists

Closed systems deal with <GermanTerm de="Volumenänderungsarbeit" article="die" en="boundary work" />: a piston pushes on gas, the gas pushes back, integrated $-\int p\,\mathrm{d}V$ gives the work.

Open systems — turbines, compressors, pumps — don't have pistons. Instead they have shafts rotating at high speed, transferring energy into or out of the fluid. The appropriate work concept here is <GermanTerm de="technische Arbeit" article="die" en="technical work" /> $W_t$ (or its rate form, power $P$). This lesson derives the formula $W_t = \int V\,\mathrm{d}p$ and shows its relationship to $W_V$.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The technical-work formula

For a quasi-static, steady, adiabatic open-system process with negligible KE and PE:

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Technische Arbeit"
  name_en="Technical work"
  latex="w_{t,12} = \int_{p_1}^{p_2} v \, \mathrm{d}p"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "w_t", name_de: "spezifische technische Arbeit", name_en: "specific technical work", unit: "J/kg" },
    { symbol: "v", name_de: "spezifisches Volumen", name_en: "specific volume", unit: "m³/kg" },
    { symbol: "p", name_de: "Druck", name_en: "pressure", unit: "Pa" },
  ]}
>

Sign: $w_t > 0$ → work INTO the fluid (compressor). $w_t < 0$ → work OUT (turbine).

For multi-kg flows, multiply by mass or (more usefully) by $\dot{m}$ to get power: $P = \dot{m} w_t$.

The full form with KE and PE:

$$w_t = \int_1^2 v\,\mathrm{d}p + \tfrac{1}{2}(c_2^2 - c_1^2) + g(z_2 - z_1)$$

</FormulaCard>

### Why $\int v \, \mathrm{d}p$ and not $-\int p\,\mathrm{d}V$?

Two derivations of the same result. Closed-system boundary work is $-\int p\,\mathrm{d}V$. Open systems add the *flow work* $pV$ to push matter across the boundary. Differentiating:

$$\mathrm{d}(pV) = p\,\mathrm{d}V + V\,\mathrm{d}p$$

So $-p\,\mathrm{d}V - \mathrm{d}(pV) = -2 p\,\mathrm{d}V - V\,\mathrm{d}p$, which doesn't cleanly give $\int V\,\mathrm{d}p$ on its own. The clean derivation is via the first law for open systems: adiabatic + steady + no KE/PE gives $w_t = h_2 - h_1$, and for a reversible process, $dh = T\,\mathrm{d}s + v\,\mathrm{d}p$, so with $\mathrm{d}s = 0$ (reversible adiabat = isentropic), $dh = v\,\mathrm{d}p$ — integrating gives $w_t = \int v\,\mathrm{d}p$.

### Evaluating $w_t$ for iso-processes

| Process | $w_t$ (per unit mass, ideal gas) |
| --- | --- |
| isobar | 0 (no pressure change → no technical work) |
| isotherm | $R_s T \ln(p_2/p_1)$ — same magnitude as $-w_V$ |
| isentrop (adiabat reversibel) | $\dfrac{\kappa}{\kappa - 1} R_s (T_2 - T_1)$ or $h_2 - h_1$ |
| polytrop ($n \neq 1$) | $\dfrac{n}{n - 1} R_s (T_2 - T_1)$ |

Note the recurring factor $\frac{n}{n-1}$ (or $\frac{\kappa}{\kappa-1}$ for isentropic). Compared to the closed-system boundary work formula $\frac{R_s(T_2-T_1)}{n-1}$, the technical work is **$n$ times larger** in magnitude.

<Callout variant="note">

The factor $n$ (or $\kappa$): technical work on a gas compression is $n \times$ the boundary work, because technical work includes both the compression energy AND the flow-work contribution needed to push the fluid across the system boundary. This is the precise sense in which $H$ (which bundles $U$ and $pV$) is the natural variable for open systems.

</Callout>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Luft wird durch einen adiabaten Verdichter isentrop von $p_1 = 1\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_1 = 300\,\mathrm{K}$ auf $p_2 = 5\,\mathrm{bar}$ verdichtet. Gas: ideal, $\kappa = 1{,}4$, $R_s = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$, $c_p = 1005\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$.
>
> *a)* Berechnen Sie $T_2$.
> *b)* Berechnen Sie die spezifische technische Arbeit $w_t$ über $\int v\,\mathrm{d}p$.
> *c)* Verifizieren Sie: $w_t = \Delta h = c_p (T_2 - T_1)$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Temperature.**

Isentropic: $T_2/T_1 = (p_2/p_1)^{(\kappa-1)/\kappa} = 5^{0{,}4/1{,}4} = 5^{0{,}2857} \approx 1{,}584$.

$T_2 \approx 300 \cdot 1{,}584 \approx 475{,}2\,\mathrm{K}$.

**b) Technical work via** $\int v\,\mathrm{d}p$.

Using the polytropic form (with $n = \kappa = 1{,}4$):

$$w_t = \frac{\kappa}{\kappa-1} R_s(T_2 - T_1) = \frac{1{,}4}{0{,}4} \cdot 287 \cdot 175{,}2 = 3{,}5 \cdot 287 \cdot 175{,}2$$

$$w_t \approx 176\,000\,\mathrm{J/kg} = 176\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$$

Positive → work INTO the fluid ✓.

**c) Cross-check with** $w_t = c_p \Delta T$.

$w_t = 1005 \cdot 175{,}2 \approx 176\,100\,\mathrm{J/kg} = 176{,}1\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$. ✓

Both approaches agree within rounding. This is the fundamental identity:

$$w_t = c_p (T_2 - T_1) = \Delta h \quad \text{for adiabatic reversible (isentropic) flow}$$

For a 1 kg/s mass-flow rate, this is 176 kW of shaft power — what the motor driving the compressor must deliver.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For open-system Klausur problems, if the device is adiabatic + steady + no KE/PE (most textbook devices), collapse the full open-system first law down to just:

$$P = \dot{m}\,\Delta h$$

Compressor or pump: $\Delta h > 0$, $P > 0$, motor supplies power.

Turbine: $\Delta h < 0$, $P < 0$, turbine delivers power.

Find $\Delta h$ from tables (for water, refrigerants) or from $c_p \Delta T$ (for air, at moderate conditions).

</Callout>
