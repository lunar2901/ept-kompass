## Why this concept exists

Previous lessons quoted the boundary-work formulas. This lesson **derives** them, so you can reconstruct the right formula on a Klausur even if you forget which one belongs to which iso-process. And it introduces the <GermanTerm de="Polytrope" article="die" en="polytropic process" /> — the general $pV^n = \mathrm{const}$ family that includes isothermal, isobaric, isochoric, and isentropic as special cases.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The master integral

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Volumenänderungsarbeit (quasistatisch, geschlossenes System)"
  name_en="Boundary work (quasi-static, closed system)"
  latex="W_{V,12} = -\int_{V_1}^{V_2} p(V) \, \mathrm{d}V"
>

Sign convention: work done ON the system is positive. For expansion ($V_2 > V_1$), the integral is positive, so $W_V$ is negative — work done BY the system.

The integral depends on $p(V)$ — i.e. on the process path. Different processes give different $W_V$ for the same endpoints.

</FormulaCard>

### Isochoric: $V = \mathrm{const}$

$\mathrm{d}V = 0$ everywhere along the path, so:

$$W_V = 0$$

No piston motion, no boundary work.

### Isobaric: $p = \mathrm{const}$

Pull $p$ out of the integral:

$$W_V = -p \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \mathrm{d}V = -p(V_2 - V_1) = -p\,\Delta V$$

Using ideal-gas law, $p\Delta V = mR_s\Delta T$, so equivalently $W_V = -mR_s\Delta T$.

### Isothermal: $T = \mathrm{const}$, ideal gas

From $pV = mR_sT$ with $T$ constant: $p = mR_sT / V$. Plug in:

$$W_V = -\int_{V_1}^{V_2} \frac{mR_sT}{V} \mathrm{d}V = -mR_sT \int_{V_1}^{V_2} \frac{\mathrm{d}V}{V} = -mR_sT \ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right)$$

Equivalent forms:

$$W_V = mR_sT \ln\!\left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right) = mR_sT \ln\!\left(\frac{p_2}{p_1}\right)$$

(The second uses Boyle's law $V_1/V_2 = p_2/p_1$ at constant $T$.)

### Polytropic: $pV^n = \mathrm{const}$

The general parametrisation. Specific values of $n$ recover the earlier cases:

| $n$ | Process |
| :---: | --- |
| $n = 0$ | isobar ($p = \mathrm{const}$) |
| $n = 1$ | isotherm (for ideal gas: $pV = \mathrm{const} \Leftrightarrow T = \mathrm{const}$) |
| $n = \kappa$ | isentrop (for ideal gas) |
| $n \to \infty$ | isochor ($V = \mathrm{const}$) |

For real compressors, $n$ typically falls between 1 and $\kappa$ — between the isothermal lower bound and the isentropic ideal limit.

<FormulaCard
  name_de="Volumenänderungsarbeit einer Polytrope (n ≠ 1)"
  name_en="Boundary work of a polytrope (n ≠ 1)"
  latex="W_V = \frac{p_2 V_2 - p_1 V_1}{n - 1} = \frac{mR_s(T_2 - T_1)}{n - 1}"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "n", name_de: "Polytropenexponent", name_en: "polytropic exponent", unit: "—" },
  ]}
>

Derivation: $p V^n = p_1 V_1^n$, so $p = p_1 V_1^n / V^n$. Integrate:

$$W_V = -p_1 V_1^n \int_{V_1}^{V_2} V^{-n}\mathrm{d}V = -p_1 V_1^n \cdot \frac{V_2^{1-n} - V_1^{1-n}}{1-n} = \frac{p_2 V_2 - p_1 V_1}{n - 1}$$

Special case $n = 1$ (isotherm) must be handled separately because the formula has a $1/0$; the log form $W_V = -mR_sT\ln(V_2/V_1)$ applies.

</FormulaCard>

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** Ein Verdichter verdichtet polytrop ($n = 1{,}3$) 0,5 kg Luft ($R_s = 287\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$) von Zustand 1 ($p_1 = 1\,\mathrm{bar}$, $T_1 = 290\,\mathrm{K}$) auf $p_2 = 6\,\mathrm{bar}$. Berechnen Sie $T_2$ und $W_V$.

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**T_2 from polytropic relation.**

For any polytrope with index $n$ on an ideal gas: $T V^{n-1} = \mathrm{const}$, or equivalently $T \, p^{(1-n)/n} = \mathrm{const}$.

$$\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{p_2}{p_1}\right)^{(n-1)/n} = 6^{0{,}3/1{,}3} = 6^{0{,}2308}$$

$$T_2 = 290 \cdot 6^{0{,}2308} \approx 290 \cdot 1{,}500 \approx 435\,\mathrm{K}$$

**W_V from polytropic work formula.**

$$W_V = \frac{m R_s (T_2 - T_1)}{n - 1} = \frac{0{,}5 \cdot 287 \cdot (435 - 290)}{1{,}3 - 1}$$

$$W_V = \frac{0{,}5 \cdot 287 \cdot 145}{0{,}3} = \frac{20\,808}{0{,}3} \approx 69\,360\,\mathrm{J} \approx 69{,}4\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**Sign.** Positive: compression, work INTO the gas. ✓

**Interpretation.** The polytropic index $n = 1{,}3$ is between $1$ (isotherm, minimum work) and $\kappa = 1{,}4$ (isentropic, maximum adiabatic work for air). The real compressor is partially cooled during compression, falling between the two limits.

</details>

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** For polytropic problems, write down the three forms of the polytropic relation and pick the one that matches your given variables:

- $pV^n = p_1 V_1^n$
- $TV^{n-1} = T_1 V_1^{n-1}$
- $T^n p^{1-n} = T_1^n p_1^{1-n}$

Isentropic: replace $n$ with $\kappa$. Isothermal ($n = 1$): these reduce to $pV = \mathrm{const}$ and $T = \mathrm{const}$.

</Callout>
