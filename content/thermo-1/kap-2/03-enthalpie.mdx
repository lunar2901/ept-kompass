## Why this concept exists

Internal energy $U$ is great for closed systems — matter stays put, so you only track what happens inside the boundary. But the moment matter crosses a boundary (a turbine, a heat exchanger, a compressor), you need to track the energy carried IN with the flowing matter, plus the work spent pushing it across the boundary. That combination is <GermanTerm de="Enthalpie" article="die" en="enthalpy" />:

$$
H = U + pV \qquad\text{or per unit mass}\qquad h = u + pv
$$

Enthalpy is what lives on steam tables. Every open-system energy balance in the rest of this course is written in $h$, not $u$. Knowing why this combination is useful is the point of this lesson.

</Callout>

## Theorie

### The definition

$H \equiv U + pV$. By construction, $H$ is a state quantity (sum of a state quantity $U$ and the product of two state quantities $p$, $V$). Its specific form: $h = u + pv$. Units are J for $H$, J/kg for $h$.

### Why "U plus pV" is the natural open-system quantity

Picture a pipe carrying fluid across a system boundary. A small chunk of fluid of volume $v$ (per unit mass) is about to enter. For it to enter, whatever is behind it must *push* it across the boundary against the local pressure $p$. The work done by that upstream fluid on the chunk is $p \cdot v$ per unit mass — this is the **Verschiebearbeit** (flow work).

So the total energy carried across the boundary per unit mass is: the internal energy $u$ the fluid had + the flow work $pv$ spent pushing it in. That sum is $h = u + pv$ — the specific enthalpy.

This is why open-system energy balances have $\dot{m} \cdot h$ terms, not $\dot{m} \cdot u$ terms: enthalpy bundles together the fluid's internal energy AND the flow work needed to move it, into a single convenient quantity.

### Ideal-gas specialisation

For an ideal gas, $pv = R_s T$, so:

$$h = u + pv = u(T) + R_s T = h(T)$$

Enthalpy depends only on temperature for an ideal gas — same simplification as internal energy. Differentiating:

  name_de="Enthalpie und c_p"
  name_en="Enthalpy and c_p"
  latex="\mathrm{d}h = c_p(T) \, \mathrm{d}T \quad \Rightarrow \quad \Delta h = \int_{T_1}^{T_2} c_p(T)\,\mathrm{d}T \approx c_p \, \Delta T"
  variables={[
    { symbol: "c_p", name_de: "spezifische Wärmekapazität bei konstantem Druck", name_en: "specific heat capacity at constant pressure", unit: "J/(kg·K)" },
  ]}
>

For any ideal-gas process, $\Delta h = c_p \, \Delta T$. Valid even for isochoric processes, because $h$ is a state quantity and depends only on $T$ for an ideal gas.

</FormulaCard>

### Heat in isobaric closed-system processes

For a closed system undergoing an isobaric (constant-$p$) process:

- First law: $\Delta U = Q + W_V$.
- Isobaric boundary work: $W_V = -p \, \Delta V$.
- So: $Q = \Delta U - W_V = \Delta U + p\,\Delta V = \Delta U + \Delta(pV) = \Delta (U + pV) = \Delta H$.

**For isobaric closed systems, $Q = \Delta H$.** This is why $H$ was invented — to let isobaric heat calculations look like state-quantity differences.

## Musteraufgabe

> **Aufgabe.** 2 kg Luft ($c_p = 1005\,\mathrm{J/(kg\,K)}$) werden in einem offenen System (Heizregister) isobar von $T_1 = 290\,\mathrm{K}$ auf $T_2 = 340\,\mathrm{K}$ erwärmt.
>
> *a)* Wie groß ist die spezifische Enthalpieänderung $\Delta h$?
> *b)* Wie groß ist die gesamte Enthalpieänderung $\Delta H$?
> *c)* Welche Wärme $Q$ muss zugeführt werden (vernachlässigbare kin./pot. Energieänderungen)?

<details>
<summary>**Lösungsweg (English)**</summary>

**a) Specific enthalpy change.**

For ideal gas, $\Delta h = c_p \Delta T$ irrespective of process type:

$$\Delta h = 1005 \cdot (340 - 290) = 1005 \cdot 50 = 50\,250\,\mathrm{J/kg} \approx 50{,}3\,\mathrm{kJ/kg}$$

**b) Total enthalpy change.**

$$\Delta H = m \cdot \Delta h = 2 \cdot 50\,250 = 100\,500\,\mathrm{J} \approx 100{,}5\,\mathrm{kJ}$$

**c) Heat required.**

For an open-system steady-flow heating with negligible KE/PE and no shaft work:

$$\dot{Q} = \dot{m} \Delta h \quad \Rightarrow \quad Q \text{ per kg of air passing through} = \Delta h$$

For the entire 2 kg of air: $Q = \Delta H = 100{,}5\,\mathrm{kJ}$.

**Comparison to closed-system isobaric heating.** Coincidentally, for an isobaric closed system with the same air, same $T_1$, $T_2$, we'd also get $Q = m c_p \Delta T = 100{,}5\,\mathrm{kJ}$. This is not a coincidence — it is the deep reason $H$ is useful in both contexts.

</details>

This table is worth memorising. It answers: "given the process type, what's the heat?" — which is the single most common Klausur sub-question.

**Open vs closed — why the same $Q = \\Delta H$ result holds for isobaric flow.**

For an open system (heat exchanger, boiler stage) operating at constant pressure with no shaft work and negligible KE/PE, the energy balance per unit mass of working fluid is:

$$\\dot{Q}/\\dot{m} = \\Delta h$$

— the same shape as $Q = \\Delta H$ for closed systems. Not coincidence: in both cases, all heat added at constant pressure shows up as an enthalpy increase, because the expansion work is already bundled into $h = u + pv$.

**Calorimetry in chemistry.** Chemical thermodynamics works in constant-$p$ conditions (open beakers), so $\\Delta H_{\\text{rxn}}$ is the natural reaction-heat measurement. Chemists call it "enthalpy of reaction" precisely because $Q_p = \\Delta H$. Engineering uses the same terminology.

<Callout variant="exam-tip">

**Klausur-Tipp.** Writing down the first law at the start of every first-law problem is worth one mark. For closed systems: $\Delta U = Q + W$. For steady-state open systems: $\dot{Q} + P_t = \dot{m}\left[\Delta h + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta c^2 + g\Delta z\right]$. Once written, the examiner knows you know the right tool — even if the algebra later goes wrong.
